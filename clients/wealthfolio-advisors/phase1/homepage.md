# Important Retirement Decisions Most People Don't Realize They're Making

A retirement transition is the process of converting decades of accumulated savings into reliable, tax-efficient income that lasts 20 to 30 years. It involves a series of financial decisions — many of them one-time and irreversible — including what to do with a 401(k), whether to take a pension lump sum or monthly income, when to claim Social Security, and whether to do Roth conversions. The choices you make in the two to five years before and after retirement have more long-term impact than almost any investment decision you'll make during your working years.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Most retirement decisions — pension elections, rollover choices, Social Security timing — cannot be undone once made.
- The order in which you draw from different retirement accounts directly affects how long your money lasts and how much you pay in taxes.
- Claiming Social Security at age 62 versus age 70 can produce a difference of more than 75 percent in your monthly benefit.
- Roth conversions may be most effective in the years between retirement and age 73, before required minimum distributions increase taxable income — though timing depends on individual tax circumstances.
- A structured retirement transition plan covers income, taxes, benefits, and estate decisions — not just investments.
- Pre-retirees between ages 55 and 70 face the highest concentration of high-stakes, time-sensitive financial decisions.

## The Six Decisions That Shape Your Retirement

Most people spend decades focused on saving — contributing to a 401(k), building an IRA, accumulating a pension. The transition from saving to spending involves a different set of decisions, many of which are made once and carry permanent consequences.

### What to Do With Your Old 401(k)

When you leave an employer, you generally have four options for your 401(k): leave it in the current plan, roll it into an IRA, roll it into a new employer's plan, or take a distribution. Each option carries different tax implications, investment access, and flexibility trade-offs. [Learn about 401(k) rollover and distribution decisions →](/old-401k-decisions/)

### Retirement Income Planning

Deciding which accounts to draw from — and in what order — is one of the most consequential choices you'll make in retirement. The sequencing of withdrawals from taxable, tax-deferred, and tax-free accounts affects your lifetime tax bill and how long your money lasts. [Explore retirement income planning decisions →](/retirement-income-decisions/)

### Roth Conversion Considerations

A Roth conversion moves money from a traditional IRA or 401(k) into a Roth account, where future growth is tax-free. The window between retirement and age 73 — before required minimum distributions begin — is often cited as a planning opportunity. Converting too much in a single year can push you into a higher tax bracket or trigger Medicare premium surcharges. [Understand Roth conversion timing →](/roth-conversion-considerations/)

### Pension Distribution Decisions

If you have a pension, you will typically be asked to choose between a lump sum and monthly income. This is usually an irrevocable decision. Factors that influence the choice include your health, life expectancy, marital status, other income sources, and risk tolerance. [Review pension distribution decision factors →](/pension-distribution-decisions/)

### Social Security Timing

You can claim Social Security as early as age 62 or as late as age 70. The difference in monthly benefit between those two endpoints can exceed 75 percent. The right timing depends on factors including your health, other income, marital status, and whether you are still working. [See Social Security timing considerations →](/retirement-income-decisions/)

### Retirement Transition Mistakes

Many of the most costly retirement mistakes are not dramatic — they are quiet decisions made without full information. Understanding where the risks are concentrated helps you ask better questions before you commit. [See common retirement transition mistakes →](/retirement-transition-mistakes/)

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a retirement transition?

A retirement transition is the shift from spending your working years accumulating savings to drawing on those savings as income. It is not a single event — it is a process involving a series of financial decisions made over several years. These decisions include what to do with employer retirement accounts, when to claim Social Security, how to draw income from different account types, whether to make Roth conversions, and how to coordinate Medicare and estate planning. Many of these decisions are made once and cannot be changed.

### What should I do with my 401(k) when I retire?

When you retire or leave an employer, you generally have four options for your 401(k): leave it in your former employer's plan, roll it into an IRA, roll it into a new employer's plan if available, or take a cash distribution. Each option has different tax implications, investment access, and flexibility. Distributions taken before age 59½ may be subject to a 10 percent early withdrawal penalty in addition to ordinary income tax. The right choice depends on your situation — a financial professional can help you evaluate the options.

### When should I claim Social Security?

Social Security can be claimed between ages 62 and 70. Benefits increase for each month you delay, and waiting until age 70 can result in a monthly benefit more than 75 percent higher than claiming at 62. Factors that affect the timing decision include your health, life expectancy, other income sources, marital status, and whether you or a spouse have dependent benefits available. There is no single answer that applies to everyone.

### What is the difference between a pension lump sum and monthly income?

A pension lump sum is a one-time payment of the full present value of your pension benefit. Monthly income provides a set payment for the rest of your life — and sometimes your spouse's life, depending on the option selected. This choice is typically irrevocable. Factors that influence which option may be more appropriate include your health, life expectancy, marital status, other income sources, risk tolerance, and investment experience. Because this decision cannot be undone, it is one that benefits from careful review.

### What is a Roth conversion, and is it worth considering?

A Roth conversion is the process of moving money from a traditional IRA or 401(k) — where contributions were made pre-tax — into a Roth account, where future qualified withdrawals are tax-free. The converted amount is treated as taxable income in the year of the conversion. Whether a Roth conversion is appropriate depends on your individual tax situation, including your current and expected future tax brackets, Medicare premium exposure, and estate planning goals. Consult a tax professional before proceeding with a Roth conversion strategy.

### What are required minimum distributions?

Required minimum distributions (RMDs) are mandatory annual withdrawals from traditional IRAs, 401(k)s, and most other tax-deferred retirement accounts. Under current rules established by the SECURE 2.0 Act, RMDs generally begin at age 73. Roth IRAs are not subject to RMDs during the account owner's lifetime. Failing to take an RMD results in a significant IRS penalty — consult a tax professional for current penalty rates and any correction options that may apply to your situation.

### What retirement mistakes should I avoid?

Common areas where people benefit from additional planning include: claiming Social Security before understanding the long-term impact on monthly income; selecting a pension option without fully evaluating the lump sum alternative; failing to update beneficiary designations after major life changes; making Roth conversions without accounting for Medicare premium thresholds; and not having a plan for required minimum distributions before they begin. Many of these situations are not obvious until the decision point has already passed.
