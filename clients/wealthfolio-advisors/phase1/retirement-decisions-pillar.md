## Direct Answer

Retirement is not a single financial event — it is a transition involving a series of decisions, many of them one-time and permanent. What you do with a 401(k), how you structure pension income, when you claim Social Security, whether to convert to Roth, and how to create reliable income are all decisions made within a few years of each other. Understanding them together produces better outcomes than addressing each in isolation.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Retirement involves multiple distinct decisions — pension elections, rollover choices, Social Security timing, income structure, and tax planning — that interact with each other
- Many retirement decisions are irreversible — pension elections, Social Security timing, and early withdrawal choices cannot be undone
- The order in which you address these decisions matters as much as each individual choice
- Retirement transition planning is different from accumulation-phase financial planning — the tools, stakes, and priorities are different
- Most people facing retirement have never faced these decisions before — they are made once, without a practice run

## Why Retirement Transition Decisions Are Different

During the working years, financial mistakes generally have recovery paths. You can increase contributions, adjust your allocation, or change course after a bad year. The accumulation phase rewards consistency more than precision.

Retirement decisions often don't have the same recovery room. A pension election made at retirement is typically permanent. Social Security timing locks in a base benefit for life. The order in which you draw from accounts shapes your tax situation for decades. Rolling over a 401(k) improperly can trigger taxes and penalties that can't be reversed. These aren't errors you catch in the next quarterly statement — they are structural decisions with long-term consequences.

That combination — multiple high-stakes decisions, compressed timeline, irreversibility — is what makes retirement transition planning its own category. The skills and habits that served you well during the saving years (contribute regularly, invest broadly, stay patient) are not the same skills this phase requires. Understanding which decisions are in front of you, what each one involves, and how they connect is the first step.

## The Decisions Most People Face

### Your Old 401(k): What to Do When You Leave an Employer

When you separate from an employer, your 401(k) doesn't disappear — it requires a decision. Leaving the money in the plan, rolling it to an IRA, rolling it to a new employer's plan, or taking a distribution are each legitimate paths. The right one depends on factors including your age, tax situation, investment options, and whether you have other accounts to coordinate. Some choices have tax implications that play out immediately; others shape your options for years.

### TSP Decisions for Federal Employees

The Thrift Savings Plan has its own distribution rules, investment options, and withdrawal mechanics that differ from a private-sector 401(k). Federal employees approaching retirement face decisions about whether to leave TSP funds in the plan, roll them to an IRA, or take distributions — and those decisions interact with pension income, Social Security timing, and Roth conversion planning. The TSP's unique structure makes it worth understanding on its own terms.

### Retirement Income Planning: Turning Savings Into Income

At retirement, the central challenge shifts from building savings to drawing them down — sustainably, tax-efficiently, and in the right order. Which accounts to draw from first, when to claim Social Security, how to protect against early market losses, and how to manage required minimum distributions are all part of income planning. The sequence of withdrawals often matters as much as the total saved.

### Roth Conversion Considerations

A Roth conversion moves money from a pre-tax account — a traditional IRA or 401(k) — to a Roth account, where future growth and qualified withdrawals are tax-free. The window between retirement and age 73, before required minimum distributions increase taxable income, is often identified as a planning opportunity. But converting too much in a single year can push income into a higher tax bracket or trigger Medicare premium surcharges. Timing and amount matter.

### Pension Distribution Decisions

If you have a pension, you will typically choose how to receive it — as a monthly lifetime income payment or as a lump sum. This decision is usually irrevocable once made. Neither option is universally better. The right choice depends on your health, your spouse's situation, your other income sources, and your risk tolerance. It should be evaluated as part of your full retirement picture, not in isolation.

### Retirement Transition Mistakes to Avoid

Many of the most costly retirement mistakes aren't dramatic — they're quiet decisions made under deadline pressure without full information. Missing a retiree medical enrollment window. Taking a pension election without modeling the break-even. Rolling over a 401(k) without understanding the tax treatment. Understanding where the risks are concentrated helps you ask better questions before decisions become permanent.

## How These Decisions Connect

The retirement decisions listed above are not independent. How you handle your 401(k) affects your future required minimum distributions. Pension income changes the Roth conversion math — if a pension provides substantial monthly income, the window and amount that make sense for a conversion may shift significantly. Social Security timing affects your tax bracket in retirement, which in turn affects how much room you have for Roth conversions before income thresholds become relevant.

The goal of this site is to help you see these connections before you're in the middle of making the decisions — not after. Most people approaching retirement are dealing with these choices for the first time. There is no practice run. The time to understand how the pieces fit is before any one of them is locked in.

This is also why the order of decisions matters. In many cases, the pension election comes first — and it carries tax and income implications that should inform the 401(k) rollover decision, the Social Security timing decision, and the Roth conversion decision that follow. Treating these as a sequence rather than a list makes each individual decision easier to evaluate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are the most important decisions to make before retirement?

The decisions that tend to carry the most long-term weight are: pension distribution elections (if you have a pension), Social Security timing, retirement account rollover or distribution choices, and the early structure of your retirement income plan. These are interconnected — how you handle one affects the others. Many of them are also irreversible, which raises the stakes for getting them right before they're finalized.

### How long before retirement should I start planning?

The transition planning window that matters most is typically two to five years before your target retirement date. That is when most of the key decisions begin to become actionable — pension elections, Social Security strategy, Roth conversion opportunities, and healthcare bridge planning all benefit from being addressed before a retirement date is set in stone. Waiting until the final months before retirement means making consequential decisions under time pressure, with less room to adjust.

### What is the difference between retirement planning and retirement transition planning?

Retirement planning, in the traditional sense, is about accumulation — saving enough over a working lifetime to retire. Retirement transition planning addresses what happens next: how to convert what you've accumulated into reliable income, what to do with the various retirement accounts and benefits you've built up, and how to sequence a series of one-time decisions in a way that doesn't create problems later. The tools, stakes, and priorities are genuinely different. The discipline you applied to accumulation doesn't automatically transfer to the distribution phase.

### What retirement decisions are typically irreversible?

Pension elections are typically irrevocable once payments begin — the payout option you choose at retirement is usually permanent. Social Security timing is also largely irreversible; while there is a narrow window to suspend or withdraw a benefit, the timing decision effectively locks in a base benefit for life. Early IRA or 401(k) distributions taken before age 59½ trigger penalties and taxes that can't be recovered. Retiree medical enrollment windows, once missed, may close permanently. These are the decisions that deserve the most careful preparation.

### What happens if I make the wrong retirement decision?

It depends on which decision. Some mistakes reduce long-term income — for example, claiming Social Security earlier than optimal means a permanently lower monthly benefit for the rest of your life. Others create immediate tax consequences — like an indirect rollover that misses the 60-day window and becomes a taxable distribution. Some mistakes compound over time in ways that don't become visible for years. The [retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/) page covers the most common errors and how they play out.

### Where do I start if I'm approaching retirement and don't know what to do first?

Start by taking inventory of what you have: retirement accounts, a pension if applicable, expected Social Security benefit, and any other income sources. Then identify which decisions have the nearest deadlines — pension elections and retiree medical enrollment windows often arrive first. From there, look at how your income sources interact. The [Retirement Transition Checklist](/retirement-transition-checklist/) is a practical starting point, and a [Retirement Transition Review](/request-review/) can help you map what needs to be addressed and in what order.
