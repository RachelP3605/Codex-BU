# Retirement Transition Checklist: 12 Decisions Most People Don't Realize They'll Face

The Retirement Transition Checklist is a free educational guide created by Aly McFarlane and WealthFolio Advisors, LLC. It covers 12 financial, tax, and legal decisions that arise during the transition from saving to spending — many of which are made once and carry permanent consequences. Most people spend decades focused on accumulation and arrive at retirement without a clear picture of what the transition itself requires.

## The 12 Decisions

### Decision 1: What to Do With Your Old 401(k) or 403(b)

When you leave an employer, you generally have four options for your employer retirement plan: leave it in your former employer's plan, roll it into an IRA, roll it into a new employer's plan if available, or take a cash distribution. Each option carries different tax implications, investment access, and flexibility trade-offs. Distributions taken before age 59½ may be subject to a 10 percent early withdrawal penalty in addition to ordinary income taxes. Understanding each option before making a selection helps avoid unintended tax consequences.

### Decision 2: Social Security Timing

Social Security benefits can be claimed as early as age 62 or as late as age 70. The monthly benefit increases for each month you delay, and waiting until age 70 can result in a significantly higher monthly benefit than claiming at 62, based on SSA benefit calculations — the exact difference depends on individual earnings history. Factors that affect the timing decision include your health, life expectancy, other income sources, marital status, and whether coordination with a spouse's benefit is relevant. There is no single answer that applies to all situations.

### Decision 3: Pension Lump Sum or Monthly Income

If you have a pension, you will typically be required to choose between a lump sum payment and monthly income. This decision is almost always irrevocable — the option selected at retirement cannot be changed later. Factors that influence which approach may be more appropriate include your health, life expectancy, marital status, other sources of income, risk tolerance, and investment experience. Because the consequences are permanent, this decision benefits from careful review — ideally with professional guidance before the election date. [Pension distribution decision factors →](/pension-distribution-decisions/)

### Decision 4: Building a Sustainable Retirement Income Plan

A retirement income plan addresses where your monthly income will come from and in what order different accounts will be drawn down. The sequence in which you draw from taxable, tax-deferred, and tax-free accounts affects your lifetime tax bill and how long your money lasts over a 20 to 30 year horizon. Without a sequencing plan, withdrawals often default to patterns that may not account for long-term tax efficiency. [Retirement income planning decisions →](/retirement-income-decisions/)

### Decision 5: Roth Conversions

A Roth conversion moves money from a traditional IRA or 401(k) — where contributions were made pre-tax — into a Roth account, where future qualified withdrawals are tax-free. The window between retirement and age 73, before required minimum distributions begin, is often cited as a planning opportunity because income may be lower than in working years. Converting too much in a single year can push you into a higher tax bracket or trigger Medicare premium surcharges known as IRMAA. Consulting a tax professional to evaluate whether a Roth conversion aligns with your overall plan is one way to assess whether this approach is relevant to your situation. [Roth conversion timing and considerations →](/roth-conversion-considerations/)

### Decision 6: Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

Under the SECURE 2.0 Act, required minimum distributions from traditional IRAs, 401(k)s, and most other tax-deferred retirement accounts must begin at age 73. Roth IRAs are not subject to RMDs during the account owner's lifetime. Failing to take an RMD in a given year results in a significant IRS penalty. Planning for RMDs before they begin — including how they interact with Social Security income and Roth conversion strategy — is an area where advance planning makes a meaningful difference. Consult a tax professional for current penalty rates and any correction options that may apply.

### Decision 7: Medicare Enrollment Timing

Medicare enrollment generally begins at age 65. Missing the initial enrollment window can result in permanent premium increases that apply for as long as you have Medicare coverage. If you are still working and covered under an employer health plan at 65, different rules apply. Individuals with higher income may also be subject to IRMAA surcharges on Medicare Part B and Part D premiums. Consult Medicare.gov or a licensed benefits counselor for current enrollment windows, penalty information, and income thresholds.

### Decision 8: Long-Term Care Planning

Medicare generally does not cover ongoing long-term custodial care — the kind of care needed if a chronic illness, disability, or cognitive decline requires assistance with daily activities for an extended period. Options for addressing this risk include traditional long-term care insurance, hybrid life insurance and annuity products with long-term care riders, self-funding through dedicated savings, and — in certain circumstances — Medicaid. Each option involves trade-offs related to cost, coverage, health qualification, and asset protection. This is an area that benefits from review before the need arises.

### Decision 9: Tax Strategy for Withdrawals

The conventional guidance for withdrawal sequencing — drawing from taxable accounts first, then tax-deferred, then tax-free — is a general framework, not a universal rule. The optimal sequence for your situation depends on your specific tax circumstances, including your current and projected brackets, the size of your traditional IRA, expected Social Security income, RMD amounts, and estate planning goals. A tax professional can help evaluate the trade-offs and identify a withdrawal approach that is consistent with your overall plan.

### Decision 10: Estate Planning and Beneficiary Designations

Retirement accounts — IRAs, 401(k)s, annuities — pass to heirs through beneficiary designations, not through your will. Outdated designations — naming an ex-spouse, a deceased individual, or failing to name a contingent beneficiary — can cause assets to pass in unintended ways. Reviewing and updating beneficiary designations is a routine but frequently overlooked step, particularly after major life events such as marriage, divorce, or the death of a named beneficiary.

### Decision 11: Mortgage Payoff

Whether to pay off a mortgage before or during retirement is both a mathematical and a personal decision. Factors that affect the analysis include the interest rate on the mortgage, the potential return on invested funds, your income in retirement, the tax treatment of mortgage interest in your specific situation, and your personal preference for having a paid-off home. Evaluating the mortgage payoff question in the context of your overall retirement income and liquidity plan provides a more complete picture than looking at it in isolation.

### Decision 12: Withdrawal Sequencing Across Account Types

The long-term efficiency of your retirement income plan depends in part on the order in which you draw from different account types — taxable, tax-deferred, and tax-free — across what may be a 20 to 30 year period. The sequence that minimizes lifetime taxes may differ from the sequence that maximizes legacy, or that best manages bracket exposure during the years before RMDs begin. Decisions about sequencing are best made as part of a comprehensive retirement income plan that accounts for your full picture: Social Security timing, RMD projections, Roth conversion strategy, and estate goals. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is included in the Retirement Transition Checklist?

The checklist covers all 12 retirement transition decision areas: what to do with your old 401(k) or 403(b), Social Security timing, pension lump sum versus monthly income, building a sustainable income plan, Roth conversion considerations, required minimum distributions, Medicare enrollment timing, long-term care planning, tax strategy for withdrawals, estate planning and beneficiary designations, mortgage payoff, and withdrawal sequencing across account types.

### Who created the Retirement Transition Checklist?

### Is the checklist free?

Yes. The Retirement Transition Checklist is offered at no cost. There is no obligation to take any further action. It is an educational resource designed to help individuals approaching or recently entering retirement understand the full range of decisions they face.

### What is the most important decision on the checklist?

Different decisions carry different weight depending on your specific circumstances. Decisions that are typically irreversible — such as pension elections and Social Security timing — often receive particular attention because the consequences of those choices extend for the remainder of your lifetime. However, the checklist is designed to help you understand the full range of decisions so you can prioritize based on your own situation. The goal is not to identify one universal answer, but to ensure you are aware of all the decisions before they are made for you by default.

### What decisions do I need to make when I retire?

The decisions that arise around retirement include: what to do with your employer retirement plan, when to claim Social Security, how to structure pension income, whether to do Roth conversions, when and how to draw from different account types, how to coordinate Medicare, whether to buy long-term care coverage, and how to structure estate documents and beneficiary designations. The checklist covers all 12 of these areas in detail.

### What is the Roth conversion window?

The Roth conversion window is a term used to describe the period — often the years between retirement and age 73, when required minimum distributions begin — during which some individuals may have lower taxable income than they did during their working years or will have once RMDs are required. For some people, this creates an opportunity to move money from a traditional IRA into a Roth account at a lower marginal tax rate than they might otherwise face. Whether a Roth conversion makes sense in a given year depends on individual circumstances, including your current tax bracket, expected future income, Medicare premium exposure, and estate planning goals. Consult a tax professional regarding your specific situation before making conversion decisions.
