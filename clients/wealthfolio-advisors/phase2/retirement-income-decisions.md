Retirement income planning means converting accumulated savings into a predictable, tax-efficient income stream that lasts as long as you do. The core challenge is sequencing: which accounts to draw from first, when to claim Social Security, and how to protect against a bad market early in retirement. Most people with multiple account types have more flexibility than they realize — but only if withdrawals are coordinated intentionally. Drawing equally from everything is rarely the optimal approach.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Retirement income planning is about sequencing withdrawals — the order matters as much as the amount you've saved
- Drawing from taxable accounts first, then tax-deferred, then Roth is a conventional starting framework — but the optimal sequence depends on your individual situation
- Delaying Social Security increases your benefit by approximately 8% per year from full retirement age to age 70
- Sequence of returns risk is highest in the first five years of retirement — a major early drop can permanently shorten how long your money lasts
- IRMAA surcharges can raise Medicare premiums significantly if income crosses certain thresholds
- A Roth IRA has no RMDs during the owner's lifetime — a powerful tool for late-retirement flexibility and estate planning

## Why the Shift From Saving to Spending Is Harder Than Most People Expect

For 30 or 40 years, the goal was simple: save more. Every financial habit, every automatic contribution, every reinvested dividend pointed in the same direction. Then retirement arrives — and the entire framework flips.

Distribution is not the mirror image of accumulation. Saving is largely mechanical — contribute regularly, invest broadly, stay patient. Spending down requires a sequence of decisions: which account to touch first, how much to take, what the tax impact will be, and how to protect what remains. These are active, recurring choices with real consequences.

Many people underestimate this shift. They arrive at retirement with significant savings and no withdrawal plan — and default to drawing from whatever account feels most accessible. That approach often works against them in ways that don't become visible until years later.

## Your Retirement Income Sources: What You're Working With

### Social Security

Social Security is a guaranteed, inflation-adjusted income source that most retirees can claim between age 62 and age 70. Claiming early, at 62, permanently reduces your monthly benefit — potentially by as much as 30% if your full retirement age (FRA) is 67. Waiting past your FRA increases your benefit by approximately 8% per year until age 70, when the credits stop accumulating.

Depending on your total income in retirement, up to 85% of your Social Security benefit may be subject to federal income tax. This makes coordination between your benefit timing and other income sources an important planning consideration.

### Pension or Annuity

If you have a pension or an annuity that produces monthly payments, that income functions as a guaranteed floor. It arrives regardless of market conditions and does not require a withdrawal decision each month. For a detailed look at pension elections, see our guide to [pension distribution options](/pension-distribution-decisions/).

### IRA and 401(k) Withdrawals

Traditional IRAs and 401(k)s are funded with pre-tax dollars. Every dollar withdrawn is taxed as ordinary income in the year you take it. Required Minimum Distributions — mandatory annual withdrawals calculated by the IRS — begin at age 73.

Roth IRAs operate differently. Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free, and there are no RMDs during the account owner's lifetime. That combination makes Roth accounts a flexible tool, particularly in later retirement years.

### Taxable Accounts

Brokerage and investment accounts outside of retirement plans can be accessed at any age without penalty. Profits are generally taxed at long-term capital gains rates if assets have been held more than a year — typically lower than ordinary income rates for most retirees.

### Part-Time Work

For some retirees, part-time work provides supplemental income and a sense of structure. Income from work can reduce how much you need to draw from investment accounts. Note: if you claim Social Security before your full retirement age while still earning income, benefits may be temporarily reduced if earned income exceeds certain limits.

## What to Ask Before Setting Your Retirement Withdrawal Strategy

- What is my Social Security break-even age, and what would it cost me to claim early vs. delay?
- What happens to my spouse's income if I die first?
- What is my tax bracket during the gap years between retirement and RMDs?
- What mix of account types do I have — taxable, tax-deferred, and Roth?
- Am I at risk of IRMAA surcharges from large distributions or conversions?
- How exposed am I to sequence of returns risk in the first five years?
- Do I have a guaranteed income floor from a pension or annuity?

## Withdrawal Strategies: Which Accounts to Draw From First

A conventional starting framework that many planners reference is: draw from taxable accounts first, tax-deferred accounts second, and Roth accounts last. The logic is that taxable accounts generate ongoing taxable events regardless, and drawing from them first allows tax-advantaged accounts to continue compounding. Roth accounts, which grow tax-free and have no RMDs, are preserved as long as possible.

A second approach — bracket management — is less about a fixed sequence and more about annual optimization. Rather than depleting one account type before touching another, this strategy draws from multiple account types each year to keep total taxable income within a targeted range. The goal is to avoid large spikes in ordinary income that could push you into a higher bracket or trigger IRMAA surcharges.

For retirees in lower-income gap years — after stopping work but before Social Security and RMDs begin — there may be an opportunity to convert traditional IRA funds to Roth at a lower tax cost. See our guide on [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/) for the full framework.

Tax sequencing strategies depend heavily on individual income levels, tax brackets, and retirement goals — a tax professional can help you evaluate the right approach for your situation.

## Social Security Timing: When Should You Claim?

Social Security timing is one of the most consequential decisions in retirement planning — and one of the most frequently made without a full analysis.

**Claiming at 62** makes benefits available early, but at a permanently reduced level. For someone whose FRA is 67, claiming at 62 can reduce the monthly benefit by as much as 30%. That reduction applies for the rest of your life.

**Claiming at full retirement age** means receiving 100% of your earned benefit with no reduction and no delayed credits.

**Claiming at 70** produces the maximum possible monthly benefit. From FRA to age 70, Social Security grows by approximately 8% per year in delayed retirement credits — a guaranteed, inflation-adjusted increase. The break-even age (when total lifetime benefits from waiting exceed early claiming) typically falls in the late 70s.

**Spouse and survivor considerations** are critical. In a married household, the higher earner's benefit becomes the survivor benefit when one spouse dies. A decision to claim early lowers not just personal lifetime income — it lowers the income floor for a surviving spouse, potentially for decades.

**Taxation of benefits.** Depending on your combined income, up to 85% of your Social Security benefit may be taxable. Coordinating the claiming decision with your overall income picture is part of building an efficient plan.

## Common Retirement Income Mistakes

**Claiming Social Security early without running a break-even analysis.** Without calculating the break-even age and considering survivor implications, the decision is incomplete.

**Drawing equally from all accounts.** This approach misses opportunities to manage tax brackets, reduce IRMAA exposure, and extend the life of Roth accounts.

**Ignoring IRMAA until it appears on a bill.** Medicare premium surcharges are calculated based on income from two years prior. Large withdrawals or conversions can trigger surcharges with a significant lag.

**Not planning for inflation.** A fixed withdrawal amount that feels comfortable today will have meaningfully less purchasing power in 15 or 20 years.

**Spending down taxable accounts too fast.** Depleting taxable accounts quickly can eliminate a flexible, favorably-taxed resource early in retirement. See [retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/) for more.

## Tax Considerations: Keeping More of What You've Saved

**Traditional IRA and 401(k) withdrawals** are taxed as ordinary income. Every dollar withdrawn adds to your taxable income for that year.

**Taxable brokerage account withdrawals** on assets held more than one year are generally taxed at long-term capital gains rates — typically lower than ordinary income rates.

**Roth IRA withdrawals** that meet qualified distribution requirements are tax-free. No federal income tax, no impact on Medicare premium calculations, no effect on Social Security taxability.

**IRMAA** surcharges add to Medicare Part B and Part D premiums when Modified Adjusted Gross Income exceeds certain thresholds that change annually. A single tax year with elevated income can produce higher Medicare premiums two years later.

**RMDs** are mandatory beginning at age 73. For retirees with large traditional account balances, RMDs can push income into higher brackets or above IRMAA thresholds whether or not the money is needed.

**Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs)** allow IRA holders aged 70½ or older to donate directly to a qualified charity from their IRA, satisfying RMD requirements without the amount counting as taxable income.

Because withdrawal tax strategies depend on your tax bracket, income sources, and long-term plan, consulting a tax professional is strongly recommended before making withdrawal sequencing decisions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much can I safely withdraw from my retirement accounts each year?

The 4% rule is a commonly cited starting point — not a guarantee of sustainability. It suggests withdrawing 4% of your portfolio in year one and adjusting for inflation annually has historically supported a 30-year retirement. Your actual sustainable rate depends on portfolio composition, market conditions, other income sources, and spending flexibility. A coordinated plan built around your actual numbers is more useful than any single-percentage rule.

### Should I take Social Security early or wait until 70?

No universal answer applies. Waiting increases your monthly benefit by approximately 8% per year from full retirement age to 70 — a guaranteed, inflation-adjusted increase. Claiming at 62 permanently reduces your benefit. The break-even age, when waiting produces more total lifetime income, typically falls in the late 70s. For married couples, the higher earner's benefit becomes the survivor's income — making the timing decision especially consequential. Health, income needs, and marital status are key variables.

### Which retirement accounts should I draw from first?

A conventional starting framework is: taxable accounts first, tax-deferred accounts second, Roth accounts last — preserving tax-advantaged growth as long as possible. However, the optimal sequence depends on your tax bracket, IRMAA exposure, Roth conversion goals, and RMD projections. Many planners recommend intentional bracket management — drawing from different account types to keep annual taxable income in an efficient range. There is no single right answer without knowing your full financial picture.

### What is sequence of returns risk and how do I protect against it?

Sequence of returns risk refers to the danger of significant investment losses in the early years of retirement while taking withdrawals. Selling shares at low prices to fund income depletes your portfolio faster and leaves fewer shares to recover when markets rebound. Strategies include keeping one to three years of income in stable assets, using guaranteed income sources to cover essential expenses, and maintaining flexibility to reduce discretionary spending in poor market years.

### Will my retirement income affect my Medicare premiums?

Yes — if your income exceeds certain thresholds, IRMAA surcharges increase Medicare Part B and D premiums. The surcharge is based on your Modified Adjusted Gross Income from two years prior. A large IRA withdrawal, Roth conversion, or asset sale can trigger higher premiums two years later. Because these thresholds change annually, proactive income management can help minimize unexpected premium increases.

### How do Required Minimum Distributions affect my retirement income plan?

RMDs from traditional IRAs and 401(k)s begin at age 73 and are mandatory. The amount is calculated based on your account balance and IRS life expectancy tables. For retirees with large traditional account balances, RMDs can push income into higher tax brackets or above IRMAA thresholds whether or not the money is needed. Roth conversions before age 73 reduce future RMD amounts by reducing the traditional account balance. Planning around RMDs is a core element of retirement income strategy.

### What is the bucket strategy for retirement income?

The bucket strategy divides retirement assets into segments based on when the money will be needed. A common version uses three buckets: short-term (cash and stable assets for one to three years of income), medium-term (more conservative investments for years four through ten), and long-term (growth-oriented for assets needed beyond ten years). The goal is to ensure near-term income is not dependent on selling equities during a market downturn — a way of managing sequence of returns risk.

### Can I reduce taxes on my retirement withdrawals?

Several coordinated strategies can reduce lifetime taxes. Sequencing withdrawals from different account types across different tax brackets is a primary tool. Strategic Roth conversions in lower-income years reduce future taxable withdrawals. Qualified Charitable Distributions allow IRA holders over 70½ to donate directly to charity, satisfying RMDs without adding to taxable income. Consulting a tax professional is strongly recommended before making withdrawal sequencing decisions.
