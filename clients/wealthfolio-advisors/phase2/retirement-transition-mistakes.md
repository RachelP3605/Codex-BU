# The Retirement Transition Mistakes That Cost People the Most — and How to Avoid Them

The most costly retirement mistakes are rarely dramatic. They come from moving too fast, not asking the right questions, or assuming the accumulation rules still apply in the distribution years. The decisions that hurt most are irreversible: claiming Social Security too early, missing the Medicare enrollment window, waiving a pension survivor benefit without understanding the tradeoff, or cashing out a retirement account and losing a significant portion to taxes and penalties. Most can be avoided — if you know they exist.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Claiming Social Security before your full retirement age permanently reduces your benefit — by up to 30% if claimed at 62 and your FRA is 67
- Missing the Medicare Part B enrollment window can result in a permanent 10% premium penalty for each 12-month period without creditable coverage
- Cashing out a retirement account before 59½ typically triggers income taxes plus a 10% early withdrawal penalty
- Failing to update beneficiary designations means accounts may pass outside your will to unintended recipients
- The 60-day indirect rollover rule is easy to violate — once you receive the funds, the clock starts
- Waiving a pension survivor benefit is irrevocable in most plans — understand the full cost before signing

## Why Retirement Mistakes Are Different: Many Are Permanent

During the accumulation years, most mistakes have a recovery path. If you undercontribute for a year or two, you can catch up later. If investments perform poorly, you have time to adjust. The math of compounding works in your favor as long as time is on your side.

The distribution phase operates by different rules. Many of the decisions you make in the years leading up to and immediately following retirement are one-way doors. You can claim Social Security early, but you generally cannot unclaim it after 12 months. You can waive a pension survivor benefit, but in most plans you cannot change it once payments begin. You can take an early distribution, but the taxes and penalty are assessed at filing — there is no do-over.

This is the shift that catches many people off guard. The habits and instincts that served you well during your working years — act quickly, simplify where possible, default to the available option — can work against you when the stakes are permanent.

## The Most Costly Retirement Transition Mistakes

### 1. Claiming Social Security Too Early and Locking In a Permanently Reduced Benefit

**Why people make it:** Social Security is available at 62, and for most people, that feels like money they've earned. After decades of payroll taxes, claiming as soon as possible can feel like the logical move.

**What it actually costs:** If your full retirement age is 67 and you claim at 62, your benefit can be reduced by up to 30%. That reduction is permanent — it applies to every check for the rest of your life. It also affects the base from which cost-of-living adjustments are calculated. For married couples, the higher earner's Social Security benefit becomes the surviving spouse's benefit when one spouse dies — claiming early can permanently reduce the income available to a surviving spouse for decades.

Delaying from full retirement age to 70 allows the benefit to grow approximately 8% per year.

**How to avoid it:** Before claiming, run a break-even analysis that accounts for your health, financial needs, and — if married — your spouse's situation and survivor income. Understand your full retirement age and what your benefit looks like at 62, at FRA, and at 70.

---

### 2. Missing the Medicare Part B Enrollment Window and Facing Lifetime Premium Penalties

**Why people make it:** Many people assume Medicare will be available with flexibility around when they sign up. The rules around employer coverage add another layer of confusion.

**What it actually costs:** If you miss your Initial Enrollment Period — the seven-month window that begins three months before your 65th birthday month — without qualifying employer coverage in place, you may face a permanent 10% late enrollment penalty on your Part B premium for each 12-month period you were eligible but did not enroll. This penalty does not expire. It applies to your Part B premium for as long as you have Medicare.

**How to avoid it:** Know your Initial Enrollment Period and mark it on your calendar before you turn 65. Confirm with HR and with Medicare whether any employer coverage qualifies as creditable coverage that permits delayed enrollment without penalty. When in doubt, contact Medicare.gov or the Social Security Administration before your IEP window closes.

### 3. Cashing Out a Retirement Account Instead of Rolling Over and Losing a Significant Portion to Taxes

**Why people make it:** When leaving a job, cashing out is often the path of least resistance. And for people facing financial pressure during a job transition, the money in a 401(k) can look like a solution to a short-term problem.

**What it actually costs:** The moment you take a distribution, 20% is withheld for federal taxes automatically. The full amount is taxable as ordinary income in the year you receive it — potentially pushing you into a higher bracket. If you are under 59½, a 10% early withdrawal penalty applies on top of that. The combined effect can eliminate 30% or more of the account balance in a single year.

**How to avoid it:** Always use a direct rollover. In a direct rollover, your old plan sends funds directly to the new IRA or plan custodian — the money never passes through your hands, no taxes are withheld, and there is no 60-day deadline. For more detail, see our page on [what to do with your old 401(k)](/old-401k-decisions/).

### 4. Ignoring Tax Planning in the Distribution Phase and Paying More Than Necessary

**Why people make it:** Many people expect tax complexity to decrease in retirement because income decreases. That assumption often turns out to be wrong.

**What it actually costs:** In retirement, income can come from multiple sources simultaneously — IRA distributions, Social Security, a pension, taxable investment accounts. Each source is taxed differently, and they interact in ways that are not always obvious. Triggering a large IRA withdrawal in a single year can push total income into a higher bracket, increase the taxability of Social Security benefits, and cross IRMAA thresholds — resulting in higher Medicare premiums.

Missing the window for Roth conversions — typically the lower-income years between retirement and when RMDs begin — is one of the more significant missed opportunities in retirement tax planning.

**How to avoid it:** Tax planning in retirement benefits from being treated as an ongoing strategy that coordinates the timing and source of withdrawals across multiple accounts. For a closer look, see our section on [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/).

### 5. Failing to Update Beneficiary Designations and Letting Assets Pass Unintentionally

**Why people make it:** Beneficiary designations are typically set once — when an account is first opened, often decades before retirement. Life changes in the intervening years, but the paperwork does not update itself.

**What it actually costs:** Retirement accounts, life insurance policies, and annuities pass directly to the named beneficiary — outside of your will, outside of probate, and outside of any estate planning documents. A valid beneficiary designation overrides everything else. In most states, courts cannot redirect assets to override a proper designation, even when it is clearly outdated.

**How to avoid it:** Review all beneficiary designations at least every few years and after any major life event — marriage, divorce, death of a family member, birth of a child or grandchild. Cover all accounts: IRAs, 401(k)s, life insurance, annuities. Name both primary and contingent beneficiaries.

### 6. Waiving a Pension Survivor Benefit Without Understanding the Permanent Cost

**Why people make it:** The comparison looks straightforward: take the full single-life benefit and receive a higher monthly check, or take the joint-and-survivor option and receive less. The monthly difference is visible and immediate. The risk the survivor benefit protects against can feel abstract.

**What it actually costs:** If the retiree dies first and no survivor benefit was elected, the spouse's pension income from that plan stops. There is no remedy after the fact. In most pension plans, the survivor benefit election is irrevocable once payments begin.

**How to avoid it:** Before evaluating any pension election, map out a complete picture of your spouse's projected income from all sources in the event you die first. Then treat the monthly reduction in pension income as the cost of insurance against that scenario. Get professional guidance before signing any pension election. For a fuller breakdown, see our section on [pension distribution options](/pension-distribution-decisions/).

### 7. Taking RMDs Incorrectly or Missing Them and Triggering IRS Penalties

**Why people make it:** RMD rules carry several nuances that trip people up. The first-year RMD can be deferred until April 1 of the following year — but doing so creates two taxable RMDs in one year. Rules differ across account types. And people who have not actively monitored account balances may not realize how significant the RMD amounts have become.

**What it actually costs:** Failing to take a full RMD triggers an IRS excise tax on the amount not withdrawn. The SECURE 2.0 Act reduced the penalty rate from its previous level — do not rely on the old 50% figure, which is outdated. The current penalty remains significant, though further reductions may apply if the error is corrected within a specific window.

RMDs are required from traditional IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s, and TSPs beginning at age 73. Roth IRAs are not subject to RMDs during the owner's lifetime.

**How to avoid it:** Begin planning for RMDs before you reach age 73. Understand which accounts are subject to RMDs and how aggregation rules apply. Track December 31 deadlines. Consult a tax professional for current penalty rates and any available correction options.

### 8. Ignoring Sequence of Returns Risk and Withdrawing at the Wrong Time

**Why people make it:** During the accumulation years, the standard advice is to stay the course through market downturns. That approach is sound while you are adding to the portfolio. It does not automatically translate to the distribution phase, where the direction of cash flow is reversed.

**What it actually costs:** When you are withdrawing from a portfolio, selling shares at depressed prices to fund living expenses depletes the account faster — and leaves fewer shares available to recover when the market rebounds. A significant market decline early in retirement has an outsized impact on a 30-year portfolio compared to the same decline in year 20.

**How to avoid it:** Keep one to three years of essential income needs in stable, liquid assets that do not require selling equities at low prices. Use guaranteed income sources — Social Security, a pension, or annuity income — to cover essential expenses. Maintain some flexibility to reduce discretionary spending in difficult years. For a fuller discussion, see our section on [retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/).

## Questions Worth Asking Before Each Major Retirement Decision

- **Is this decision reversible?** If the answer is no, or if you are not sure, that alone warrants more time before moving forward.
- **Have I accounted for my spouse or dependents if circumstances change?** Many retirement decisions affect more than one person's financial security.
- **What are the tax consequences now, and in future years?** The downstream effects — on Medicare premiums, Social Security taxability, or future RMD amounts — are less obvious and often larger.
- **Am I making this under real or perceived time pressure?** Confirm whether a deadline is real before letting it drive the choice.
- **Have I compared options against a realistic full income projection?** Retirement decisions are most clearly evaluated in the context of a complete picture.
- **Who is helping me with this analysis, and what is their interest?** Understand who is advising you and whether they are compensated in a way that aligns with your best outcome.

## Tax Mistakes Retirees Make Most Often

**Large IRA withdrawals concentrated in a single year.** Taking a significant distribution in one year can spike taxable income — pushing other income into a higher bracket, triggering IRMAA surcharges, and increasing the taxable portion of Social Security.

**Not withholding taxes from pension and IRA distributions.** Many retirees do not realize they need to elect withholding or request sufficient amounts. The result can be underpayment penalties at filing.

**Ignoring Social Security taxability.** Up to 85% of Social Security benefits can be taxable, depending on total income. This affects how other income sources should be sequenced and timed.

**Forgetting the inherited IRA 10-year rule.** Under current rules, most non-spouse beneficiaries must fully distribute inherited IRAs within 10 years. For beneficiaries in their peak earning years, this can add significant taxable income at high marginal rates.

**Missing the Qualified Charitable Distribution opportunity.** IRA holders age 70½ or older can make a QCD directly from their IRA to a qualified charity — up to the annual IRS limit. This amount is excluded from taxable income and can satisfy part or all of an RMD. It is one of the more tax-efficient strategies available for eligible IRA holders and is frequently overlooked. Consult a tax professional to determine whether a QCD is appropriate for your situation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the biggest financial mistake people make when they retire?

The most costly single mistake in many cases is claiming Social Security too early — specifically, claiming before full retirement age without running a break-even analysis or accounting for the permanent reduction in benefit. For married couples, this also reduces the surviving spouse's lifetime income. A close second is cashing out a retirement account rather than rolling it over, triggering immediate taxes and penalties. Both mistakes are avoidable with planning and a clear understanding of the consequences before the decision is made.

### Can I undo a Social Security claim if I made it too early?

There is one mechanism available: withdrawing your application. This must be filed within 12 months of first receiving benefits. You must repay all benefits received — including any benefits paid to family members on your record — and your benefit resets as if you never filed. After 12 months, the withdrawal option closes. If you are at or past full retirement age, voluntary suspension is a separate option: payments stop, your benefit accrues delayed retirement credits at approximately 8% per year under current law until you restart or reach age 70, and no repayment is required. Consult a financial or Social Security professional before pursuing either option.

### What happens if I miss the Medicare enrollment window?

If you miss your Initial Enrollment Period without qualifying employer coverage in place, you may face a permanent 10% late enrollment penalty on your Part B premium for each 12-month period you were eligible but did not enroll. This penalty applies for as long as you have Medicare coverage — it does not expire. There are valid exceptions if you have qualifying employer group health coverage while actively employed. Verify your situation with Medicare.gov or the Social Security Administration before assuming you are covered.

### How do I avoid making a mistake with my 401(k) rollover?

The most important rule is to always use a direct rollover — never an indirect rollover. In a direct rollover, your old plan sends funds directly to the new IRA or plan custodian. No money passes through your hands, no taxes are withheld, and there is no 60-day window to worry about. In an indirect rollover, 20% is withheld immediately, and you must deposit the full original amount — including the withheld 20% — within 60 days. Missing that deadline or the missing amount triggers a taxable distribution. Always specify direct rollover when initiating the transfer.

### What beneficiary designation mistakes should I watch for?

The most common errors include: not updating after a divorce or remarriage; naming a minor child as a direct beneficiary without a trust; failing to name a contingent beneficiary; and not reviewing designations after the death of a primary beneficiary. Also watch for accounts where no beneficiary is named at all — these may pass through probate, delaying distribution and potentially losing tax-advantaged status. Review all retirement accounts, life insurance policies, and annuities at least every few years and after major life events.

### Is it ever okay to cash out a retirement account?

In limited circumstances, yes. Genuine financial emergencies may warrant a distribution, provided the costs — income taxes and the 10% early withdrawal penalty before 59½ — are understood and accepted. The Rule of 55 may allow penalty-free 401(k) access if you separated from service in the year you turned 55 or later. Other narrow exceptions include disability and certain medical expenses. In most circumstances, cashing out comes at significant cost. Whether an exception applies to your situation is worth confirming with a financial or tax professional before taking a distribution.

### What is the penalty for getting an RMD wrong?

Failing to take a full Required Minimum Distribution triggers an IRS excise tax on the amount not withdrawn. The SECURE 2.0 Act reduced the penalty rate from its previous level, though the penalty remains significant. Further reductions may apply if the error is corrected within a specific window. Relief is sometimes available for reasonable errors. If you believe you have missed or underpaid an RMD, consult a tax professional promptly. The current penalty rate and correction options are best confirmed directly with a tax advisor, as the rules have changed in recent years.

### How can I find out if I'm making a retirement mistake before it's too late?

Working with an advisor who specializes in retirement transitions is one way to identify decisions you may not know you're missing. You can also review resources from the Social Security Administration (ssa.gov), IRS.gov, and Medicare.gov for guidance on specific decisions. The topics covered on this site — rollover mechanics, Social Security timing, Medicare enrollment, pension elections, beneficiary designations, and tax planning — provide a useful starting framework for self-review.
