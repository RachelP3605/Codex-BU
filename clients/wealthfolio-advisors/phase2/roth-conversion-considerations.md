## Direct Answer

A Roth conversion means paying income tax on pre-tax retirement funds today in exchange for tax-free growth and withdrawals later. The years between retirement and age 73 — before Required Minimum Distributions force taxable income — often represent the best window for conversions. Done strategically, partial annual conversions may fill lower tax brackets, reduce future RMDs, lower Medicare premiums, and create tax-free assets for heirs — depending on individual circumstances.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The gap between retirement and age 73 (when RMDs begin) is often the best window — income may be lower and bracket space is available
- Every dollar converted pays income tax now; whether it makes sense depends on your current tax rate versus your expected future tax rate
- Roth conversions can permanently reduce future RMDs, which also reduces future taxable income and potentially Medicare premiums
- IRMAA thresholds mean conversions that raise income above certain levels can increase Medicare Part B and D premiums two years later
- Roth IRAs have no RMDs during the owner's lifetime — valuable for retirement income flexibility and passing assets to heirs
- Partial conversions spread over multiple years are often more efficient than a single large conversion for those with sufficient lower-bracket space

## Why the Pre-Retirement Years Are a Unique Tax Planning Window

Most people spend their working years in relatively high tax brackets. Salary, bonuses, and investment income keep taxable income elevated year after year. Then retirement arrives — and for many people, income drops significantly before Social Security is fully claimed and before Required Minimum Distributions begin at age 73.

That gap is real. It can last anywhere from a few years to over a decade. And it represents something that does not exist at any other point in the retirement timeline: a window where income may be low, tax brackets may be more favorable, and traditional IRA balances have not yet started forcing taxable distributions.

The challenge is that this window is finite. Once RMDs begin at 73, the IRS requires withdrawals from traditional IRAs and pre-tax accounts whether you need the income or not. Those mandatory distributions add to taxable income every year.

## How a Roth Conversion Works: The Mechanics

A Roth conversion moves money from a pre-tax retirement account — a traditional IRA, rollover IRA, 401(k), or other pre-tax account — into a Roth IRA. The amount you convert is added to your taxable income for that calendar year, and you pay ordinary income tax on it at your current rate.

Once the funds are in the Roth IRA, they grow tax-free. Qualified withdrawals — generally after age 59½ and after the account has been open at least five years — come out tax-free as well. There is no annual limit on how much you can convert.

The IRS tracks Roth conversions on Form 8606. Each conversion carries its own 5-year clock, which matters for people under age 59½ who want to access converted funds before that window closes.

One additional mechanic worth understanding is the pro-rata rule. If you have both pre-tax and after-tax (non-deductible) contributions in your IRAs, the IRS treats all your IRA funds as a single pool when calculating how much of any conversion is taxable. You cannot selectively convert only the pre-tax portion and leave the after-tax portion in place.

## The RMD Window Strategy: Converting Between Retirement and Age 73

The concept behind the RMD window strategy is straightforward: use the lower-income years between retirement and age 73 to convert portions of your traditional IRA to Roth — paying tax at today's potentially lower rate rather than waiting for RMDs to force larger, taxable distributions later.

When you stop working, earned income drops or disappears. If you delay Social Security, your income in early retirement may be quite low compared to your working years. That lower income often means unused bracket space — room in your current tax bracket that a Roth conversion can fill without pushing you into a higher rate.

Consider a hypothetical couple who retires in their early 60s with a significant traditional IRA balance, delays Social Security, and has relatively modest other income in those early retirement years. Rather than letting the traditional IRA continue to grow untouched until age 73, they work with a tax professional to identify available bracket space each year and convert that amount consistently over multiple years. By the time they reach 73, their traditional IRA balance is smaller, their required minimum distributions are smaller, and they have built a meaningful Roth balance that grows tax-free and requires no distributions during their lifetimes.

This is a conceptual framework, not a specific recommendation. The right conversion amount, timing, and total approach depends entirely on your individual tax situation. A tax professional can help you model the actual numbers.

## Questions Worth Asking Before Converting

- **What is my current marginal tax rate, and what rate do I expect to pay on withdrawals in later retirement?** If your current rate is meaningfully lower than your projected future rate, a conversion may be favorable.
- **How large are my projected RMDs at age 73 if I make no conversions?** The larger the projected RMD, the more urgency there may be to reduce the traditional IRA balance.
- **How close am I to an IRMAA income threshold?** A conversion that crosses a threshold can increase Part B and D premiums for a full year — two years later.
- **Do I have after-tax contributions in any IRA?** If so, the pro-rata rule affects how the taxable portion of any conversion is calculated.
- **Are there any other significant income events expected the same year?** A large capital gain, pension lump sum, or real estate sale can interact with a Roth conversion in ways that affect the final tax bill.
- **What are my goals for these Roth assets?** Personal income flexibility, estate planning, or both — the answer affects how to prioritize size and timing.

## Common Roth Conversion Mistakes

**Converting too much in a single year.** A large single-year conversion can push taxable income into a higher bracket, trigger IRMAA surcharges, and create a larger tax bill than the conversion saves over time.

**Ignoring the two-year IRMAA lookback.** A large conversion at 63 shows up in Medicare premiums at 65. Many people are caught off guard when premiums increase.

**Not accounting for the pro-rata rule.** People with after-tax IRA contributions sometimes assume they can convert only the pre-tax money. The pro-rata rule prevents this — ignoring it leads to unexpected tax bills.

**Converting without a multi-year plan.** Sporadic conversions are rarely as efficient as a deliberate, multi-year strategy that maps out bracket space and conversion targets over several years.

**Paying conversion taxes from the converted funds themselves.** Using the converted amount to pay the tax reduces what ends up in the Roth IRA and diminishes the long-term benefit. Pay the tax from liquid savings or other non-retirement funds.

**Forgetting the 5-year conversion rule for those under 59½.** Converted funds have their own 5-year clock before they can be accessed penalty-free as principal.

## Tax Considerations: Brackets, IRMAA, and the Break-Even Question

The core tax question in any Roth conversion is whether the rate you pay today is lower than the rate you would pay on those funds in the future. If you are in a lower bracket now than you expect to be later — because of reduced income in early retirement or because future RMDs will push you into a higher bracket — the math may favor converting now.

IRMAA income thresholds change annually and should be verified at the time of planning. A Roth conversion that pushes Modified Adjusted Gross Income above one of these thresholds — even by a small amount — can increase Medicare premiums for the entire year in which that income is assessed.

Some planners operate on the assumption that future tax rates may be higher than current rates — though this is speculative and not a certainty. Whether this assumption is relevant to your situation is something a tax professional can help you evaluate.

When a Roth IRA passes to a non-spouse beneficiary, that beneficiary generally must distribute the entire account within 10 years. However, those distributions are tax-free. A beneficiary inheriting a traditional IRA faces the same 10-year rule, but every dollar distributed is taxable. For estate planning purposes, a Roth IRA can be a significantly more valuable asset to leave to heirs.

## How Roth Conversions Connect to Estate Planning and Retirement Income

A Roth IRA is unique among retirement accounts: the original owner is never required to take distributions during their lifetime. There are no RMDs. The account can grow untouched for as long as the owner lives, making it one of the most flexible assets in a retirement income plan.

That flexibility has direct income planning value. If retirement income from Social Security, RMDs, and other sources is sufficient to cover expenses, a Roth IRA can simply continue growing — available as a reserve for large expenses, healthcare, or legacy. It does not add to taxable income unless you choose to withdraw from it.

Whether your primary goal is personal flexibility, income tax management, or leaving a cleaner asset to heirs, the Roth IRA's structure supports all three. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### When does a Roth conversion actually make financial sense?

A Roth conversion makes sense when the tax rate you pay on the conversion today is expected to be lower than the rate you would pay on future withdrawals. The most favorable conditions: a lower-income period (the gap between retirement and RMD age), an expectation of higher future income from RMDs and Social Security, and the ability to pay conversion taxes from outside the IRA. Conversions can also make sense for estate planning purposes even when the personal tax math is roughly breakeven — tax-free inheritance has real value for heirs. Consult a tax professional to evaluate whether a Roth conversion aligns with your specific situation.

### How much can I convert each year without going into a higher tax bracket?

You can convert up to the amount that fills the remaining space in your current tax bracket without crossing into the next one. This requires knowing your other projected income for the year — Social Security, pension, other withdrawals, investment income — and calculating available bracket space after accounting for all of it. You also need to stay below IRMAA income thresholds if you are within two years of Medicare eligibility. The right conversion amount is calculated from your actual tax situation each year. Consult a tax professional before determining a conversion amount.

### What is the 5-year rule for Roth conversions?

There are two Roth 5-year rules. The first: to receive qualified tax-free distributions from a Roth IRA, the account must have been open at least five years and you must be at least 59½. The second applies to each individual conversion: converted funds have their own 5-year clock for penalty-free access to that converted principal — relevant if you are under 59½ at the time of conversion. Once you are past 59½ and have met the first 5-year rule, subsequent conversions generally do not create additional complications for qualified distributions. Consult a tax professional regarding your specific situation and timeline.

### Can a Roth conversion affect my Medicare premiums?

Yes. Medicare Part B and D premiums are based on Modified Adjusted Gross Income from two years prior. If a Roth conversion raises your income above an IRMAA income threshold in a given year, your Medicare premiums will increase for the full year in which that income is assessed. These thresholds change annually. This is not a reason to avoid conversions — but it is a reason to size conversions carefully in years approaching or following Medicare eligibility at age 65. Consult a tax professional regarding your specific income and premium situation before converting.

### Should I do a Roth conversion if I'm already in retirement?

This page focuses on the pre-retirement and early-retirement conversion window, but the two are closely connected. Whether you are approaching retirement or recently retired, the window between your current age and age 73 — when required minimum distributions begin — is often the key strategic period for evaluating conversions. In early retirement, income may be lower than during working years, and bracket space may be available before Social Security is fully claimed and before RMDs add to taxable income. Whether a Roth conversion makes sense in your specific situation — before or during retirement — depends on your tax bracket, income sources, and long-term plan. Consult a tax professional before initiating any conversion.

### What's the difference between a Roth contribution and a Roth conversion?

A Roth contribution is money placed directly into a Roth IRA from after-tax income — subject to annual contribution limits and income eligibility requirements. A Roth conversion is the movement of existing pre-tax retirement funds (from a traditional IRA, 401(k), or other pre-tax account) into a Roth IRA — with the converted amount treated as taxable income in the year of conversion. There are no income limits on Roth conversions. They are distinct transactions with different rules, different tax treatment, and different planning implications.

### Do Roth conversions affect Required Minimum Distributions?

Yes — indirectly, but meaningfully. RMDs are calculated based on the balance in your traditional IRA and other pre-tax accounts at the end of the prior year. Roth conversions reduce your traditional IRA balance, which means a smaller account from which future RMDs are calculated. Smaller RMDs mean less mandatory taxable income in later retirement — giving you more control over your tax picture when distributions become required. This is one of the primary long-term benefits of strategic pre-73 conversions. Consult a tax professional to model the impact on your specific projected RMD amounts.

### Can I convert my 401(k) directly to a Roth IRA?

Yes, if your plan allows direct Roth rollovers. Pre-tax 401(k) funds rolled directly to a Roth IRA are treated as taxable income in the year of the conversion. Alternatively, you can first roll the 401(k) to a traditional IRA and then convert — the tax effect is the same either way. Roth 401(k) funds can be rolled to a Roth IRA without triggering a taxable event. Check with your plan administrator about your plan's specific rollover options. Consult a tax professional before proceeding.
