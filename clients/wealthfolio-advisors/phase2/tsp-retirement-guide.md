## Direct Answer

When a federal employee retires, TSP funds don't have to be moved — the TSP offers its own withdrawal options including monthly installment payments, a life annuity, and lump-sum withdrawals. Rolling to an IRA expands investment choices and estate planning flexibility. But the TSP's low-cost G Fund and institutional investment fees are real advantages worth evaluating carefully before making any move. This is not a decision to make by default.

## Why TSP Retirement Decisions Are Different From 401(k) Decisions

The Thrift Savings Plan is administered by the Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board (FRTIB), a government agency — not a private financial institution. That distinction matters. The TSP operates on an institutional cost structure, which means administrative fees that are typically far lower than what retail mutual funds charge inside an IRA.

Federal retirement also involves multiple income sources that don't exist in a standard private-sector career: a FERS pension based on years of service and high-3 average salary, Social Security benefits (for most FERS employees), and the TSP as a third, flexible component. These three interact through your overall tax picture, which makes TSP decisions more layered than simply comparing investment menus.

The decision of what to do with a TSP at retirement doesn't exist in isolation. It connects to questions about when to take Social Security, how FERS income fills your baseline needs, and whether you'll need TSP withdrawals to bridge any gap. Those [retirement transition decisions](/retirement-decisions/) deserve a coordinated look before any single move is made.

## TSP Distribution Options in Retirement

The TSP offers several ways to access your money in retirement. Each works differently, and choosing one does not have to be permanent — except in the case of the TSP life annuity. Understanding what each option actually does is the starting point.

### Installment Payments

Installment payments allow you to receive recurring distributions from your TSP balance on a monthly, quarterly, or annual schedule. Your money stays in the TSP, continues to be invested in the funds you select, and you retain control over adjustments going forward.

You can choose a fixed dollar amount per payment or have the TSP calculate payments based on your life expectancy. Either way, the remaining balance continues to grow or fluctuate with the market. You can change the payment amount or frequency, and you can stop installment payments and choose a different distribution option later.

This flexibility makes installment payments one of the most adaptable options for retirees who want ongoing income without giving up access to their funds. It also keeps G Fund access intact.

### Lump-Sum Withdrawals

A lump-sum withdrawal means taking all or part of your TSP balance as a single payment. The full amount withdrawn is subject to ordinary federal income tax in the year you receive it, and it does not qualify for special tax treatment simply because it came from the TSP.

A partial lump sum can make sense for a one-time expense — home renovation, debt payoff, or a large purchase — without disrupting the rest of your account. Taking the entire balance as a lump sum concentrates taxable income into a single year, which can create a significant tax event.

Lump-sum withdrawals are not a long-term income strategy. They are a tool for accessing a specific amount of capital when needed, and the tax implications should be evaluated before requesting one.

### TSP Life Annuity

The TSP life annuity option converts some or all of your balance into guaranteed lifetime income. That income is purchased through a third-party insurance company contracted by the TSP — it is not paid directly from your TSP account.

This is an irrevocable decision. Once you use TSP funds to purchase an annuity, you surrender the principal. You receive a monthly payment for life, and depending on the annuity type you choose, there may be survivor or period-certain options available. But you cannot undo the purchase or access the underlying funds.

The annuity is designed to provide income for as long as you live, which is meaningful protection against longevity risk. That protection comes at the cost of flexibility and access. Before selecting this option, understanding the terms — the insurance company involved, payout structure, survivor provisions, and what you are giving up — is essential.

### Leaving Funds in TSP

Leaving your balance in the TSP after retirement is a valid, deliberate choice — not just inaction. Your account remains open, you retain access to all TSP investment funds including the G Fund, and you are not required to take any withdrawal until required minimum distributions begin at age 73 under the SECURE 2.0 Act.

For federal retirees who are drawing from their FERS pension and deferring Social Security, the TSP can serve as a reserve account during the early years of retirement. Keeping the balance growing in low-cost TSP funds while drawing from other income sources is a straightforward way to maximize the compounding period before RMDs require distributions.

## TSP Rollover to IRA: When It Makes Sense and When It Doesn't

Rolling your TSP to an IRA is the most visible retirement decision federal employees hear about — often from financial professionals who can open IRA accounts. It is worth understanding both the genuine reasons this can make sense and the reasons it may not.

**Reasons to consider rolling to an IRA:**
- A broader investment menu, including individual stocks, bonds, sector funds, and a wider range of mutual funds and ETFs than the TSP's limited fund lineup
- Consolidation of multiple retirement accounts into a single IRA for simpler management
- Roth conversion strategy — rolling traditional TSP to a traditional IRA creates flexibility to convert funds to a Roth IRA in lower-income years. See [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/) for more on this approach.
- Estate planning flexibility — IRA beneficiary rules and inherited IRA options may offer more planning tools than TSP beneficiary provisions in certain situations

**Reasons to stay in the TSP:**
- The G Fund is not available anywhere else. It invests in specially issued Treasury securities with no principal risk and interest tied to longer-term Treasury yields — a combination that does not exist in retail investment products.
- Institutional fee advantage: TSP administrative costs are among the lowest in the industry. Many IRA investments carry expense ratios that meaningfully exceed TSP fund costs, which compounds against you over time.
- Installment payment flexibility within the TSP eliminates the need to manage systematic withdrawals from an IRA.

Whether staying in the TSP or rolling to an IRA is the right fit depends on your income needs, investment preferences, and the benefits your specific plan offers.

## What to Ask Before Moving Your TSP

Before initiating any TSP rollover or withdrawal, these questions are worth working through deliberately:

- **What does my income timeline look like?** When does FERS income start? When do I plan to take Social Security? Does my TSP need to produce income immediately?
- **Do I have a specific use for the G Fund?** If capital preservation plays a role in my plan, will I have access to an equivalent once I leave the TSP?
- **What is the actual fee comparison?** What will I pay in expense ratios inside an IRA versus TSP administrative costs?
- **Do I have a Roth conversion plan?** If converting traditional TSP to Roth is part of the strategy, rolling to a traditional IRA first gives more flexibility — but the tax implications need to be modeled.
- **Are my beneficiary designations current?** TSP beneficiary designations are separate from IRA designations and separate from your will.
- **Does WEP or GPO apply to my situation?** Federal employees with certain state or local government employment history may be subject to the Windfall Elimination Provision or Government Pension Offset. See [government employee retirement planning](/government-retirement-transition-guide/) for more context.

## Common TSP Retirement Mistakes

Federal retirees face decisions that are genuinely different from private-sector retirement. See [retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/) for the full picture. TSP-specific ones worth knowing include:

**Moving without evaluating the G Fund.** Once you roll out, the G Fund is gone permanently.

**Choosing the TSP annuity without comparison shopping.** The TSP life annuity is one lifetime income option — not the only one. Selecting it before evaluating how it fits alongside FERS, Social Security, and other options can lead to a decision that is difficult to rationalize once made.

**Taking a large lump-sum distribution.** Withdrawing a significant TSP balance in a single year concentrates taxable income in that year, which can affect tax brackets, Social Security taxation, and potentially Medicare surcharges.

**Misunderstanding proportional withdrawal rules.** If you have both traditional and Roth TSP balances, TSP withdrawals are generally taken proportionally from both. You cannot withdraw only from one source and leave the other untouched within the TSP.

**Outdated or missing beneficiary designations.** TSP beneficiary designations do not automatically update when you marry, divorce, or have children.

**Not coordinating FERS, Social Security, and TSP.** Taking all three without a sequencing strategy can result in unnecessarily high taxes across your retirement years.

## How FERS, TSP, and Social Security Are Taxed Together in Retirement

**FERS pension** is taxed as ordinary income at the federal level. Most federal retirees will have contributed to FERS on an after-tax basis, meaning a small portion of each pension payment may be excluded from income — but the vast majority of FERS pension income is fully taxable.

**Traditional TSP withdrawals** are taxed as ordinary income in the year you receive them. Roth TSP qualified withdrawals — after the 5-year aging period is met and taken after age 59½ — are federal income tax-free.

**Social Security** may be partially taxable depending on your combined income. Up to 85% of Social Security benefits may be subject to federal income tax depending on your total income from all sources.

The coordination question is about sequencing: which income sources to draw from first, in which years, to minimize taxes over your full retirement. The years between retirement and age 73 — before required minimum distributions begin from the TSP — can be a window where income is lower and Roth conversions or deliberate TSP withdrawals can be made at more favorable tax rates.

TSP withdrawals can also affect income-related Medicare surcharges. IRMAA adds premium surcharges to Medicare Part B and Part D for individuals whose income exceeds certain thresholds, which change annually. A large TSP distribution in one year can push income above a threshold, resulting in higher Medicare premiums two years later. Because these interactions can be significant, consulting a tax professional before establishing your TSP withdrawal strategy is advisable.

## How TSP Fits Into Your Broader Retirement Income Plan

A useful way to think about federal retirement income is in layers:

**FERS pension is the guaranteed floor.** It arrives monthly for life, adjusted periodically for inflation, and does not depend on market performance.

**Social Security is the second layer.** For FERS employees with Social Security-covered earnings, benefits add another stream of guaranteed lifetime income.

**TSP is the flexible component.** Unlike the pension and Social Security, the TSP can be drawn on in variable amounts, left to grow, or structured as installment payments. That flexibility is its primary advantage in the income plan.

See [retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/) for more on building that full picture.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I keep my money in TSP after I retire from federal service?

Yes. The TSP remains open to separated and retired federal employees and military members. You are not required to withdraw or move your money at retirement. Required minimum distributions begin at age 73 under the SECURE 2.0 Act, but no distribution is required the day you retire. Many federal retirees draw from their FERS pension in the early years of retirement, leave the TSP intact to continue growing, and begin TSP distributions later. Keeping funds in the TSP is a deliberate, legitimate long-term strategy — not just postponing a decision.

### What's the difference between TSP installment payments and a TSP annuity?

Installment payments are recurring withdrawals from your TSP account balance. Your money stays in the TSP, remains invested in your chosen funds, and you retain control — you can change the payment amount, adjust the frequency, or stop payments altogether. A TSP life annuity is fundamentally different: you use your TSP balance to purchase a guaranteed lifetime income stream from a third-party insurance company contracted by the TSP. Once purchased, the decision is irrevocable. You receive monthly income for life but surrender the underlying principal and all future control over those funds.

### Should I roll my TSP over to an IRA when I retire?

Not necessarily — and not by default. The TSP has real structural advantages: the G Fund is a capital preservation option with Treasury-tied returns that does not exist in retail investment products, and TSP administrative costs are among the lowest available anywhere. Rolling to an IRA can make sense if you want a broader investment menu, are planning Roth conversions, want to consolidate multiple retirement accounts, or have specific estate planning goals that an IRA structure supports better. Whether staying in the TSP or rolling to an IRA is the right fit depends on your income needs, investment preferences, and the benefits your specific plan offers.

### What happens to my Roth TSP when I retire?

Your Roth TSP can remain in the TSP or be rolled to a Roth IRA. There is an important distinction: Roth TSP is subject to required minimum distributions beginning at age 73, just like traditional TSP. A Roth IRA is not subject to RMDs during the account owner's lifetime. If you want to preserve Roth assets for as long as possible — particularly funds intended for heirs — rolling Roth TSP to a Roth IRA eliminates the RMD requirement and extends the tax-free growth period. The Roth-to-Roth rollover rules and 5-year clocks should be reviewed before acting.

### When do I have to start taking RMDs from my TSP?

Under the SECURE 2.0 Act, required minimum distributions from the TSP begin at age 73. If you reached age 72 before January 1, 2023, different transition rules applied — consult a tax advisor if your situation falls in that range. The TSP calculates and notifies you of your RMD amount annually. Failing to take the full required minimum distribution results in a significant IRS excise tax on the amount not withdrawn.

### Can I roll my TSP to a Roth IRA and do a Roth conversion?

Yes. You can roll traditional TSP funds to a traditional IRA and subsequently convert those funds to a Roth IRA, or in some cases roll directly from traditional TSP to a Roth IRA — both approaches trigger the same taxable event in the year of the conversion. The converted amount is added to your ordinary income for that year. This strategy can be particularly useful in the window between retirement and age 73, when income may be lower and tax rates more favorable before RMDs begin. Consult a tax professional before executing a conversion.

### Does my TSP affect my FERS pension or Social Security?

TSP withdrawals do not directly reduce your FERS pension — your pension is calculated on years of creditable service and your high-3 average salary. TSP withdrawals also do not affect your Social Security benefit calculation, which is based on covered wage earnings over your working life. However, traditional TSP withdrawals increase your taxable income in the year you receive them. That additional income can affect what percentage of your Social Security benefit is subject to federal income tax — up to 85% depending on your combined income — and can affect income-related Medicare surcharges. Because these interactions can be significant, consulting a tax professional before establishing your TSP withdrawal strategy is advisable.

### What is the G Fund and should I stay in it in retirement?

The G Fund invests in Treasury securities specially issued to the Thrift Savings Plan by the federal government. It is designed to preserve principal while earning interest based on longer-term Treasury yields — with no market risk, no default risk, and no possibility of principal loss. This combination is not available in retail IRAs or most commercial investment products. In retirement, the G Fund can serve as a capital preservation holding for funds allocated to near-term income needs. Whether it makes sense in your specific allocation depends on your income timeline, overall asset mix, and how you are drawing from FERS and Social Security. Leaving the TSP preserves G Fund access permanently; rolling out eliminates it.
