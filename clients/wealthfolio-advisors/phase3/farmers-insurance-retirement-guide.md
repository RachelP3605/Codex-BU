## Farmers Insurance Retirement Transition Guide: Decisions Employees and Agents Commonly Face

## Direct Answer

Farmers Insurance employees and agents approaching retirement face different decisions depending on whether they are corporate employees or independent producers. Corporate employees typically navigate 401(k) rollover timing, deferred compensation elections, and retiree health coverage transitions. Agents and producers face the added complexity of winding down variable production income while managing deferred compensation, agency considerations, and Social Security timing simultaneously.

## Retirement Transition Overview

Farmers Insurance Group, operating through Farmers Group, Inc. and its affiliated companies, employs a large workforce across both corporate and agency functions. What makes retirement planning at Farmers Insurance distinctive is that this employer has two very different audiences approaching the same transition — and most retirement planning resources do not acknowledge that distinction.

Corporate employees at Farmers typically participate in employer-sponsored defined contribution plans and may have accumulated deferred compensation as well. Independent agents and producers, on the other hand, often operate with a different financial structure: variable production income, potentially a different mix of savings vehicles, and retirement considerations that may include how to wind down or transition an agency.

This guide addresses both groups — clearly and separately where the decisions diverge. It is not a summary of Farmers Insurance's benefits program. It is a decision education resource for people at or near the end of their Farmers careers who are beginning to evaluate what comes next.

## Retirement Decisions Farmers Insurance Employees and Agents Often Face

### 401(k) Rollover Decision (Corporate Employees)

For Farmers Insurance corporate employees leaving the company, one of the first decisions is what to do with assets in a 401(k) or similar defined contribution plan. The options are generally to leave the balance in the plan, roll to an IRA, take a distribution, or — in some cases — execute a partial rollover while leaving some assets in the plan.

Rolling to an IRA is often the most flexible option, giving the retiree broader investment choices and consolidated account management. But staying in the plan can have advantages depending on the plan's investment options, fee structure, and whether the retiree needs income before age 59½. Taking a full distribution triggers ordinary income tax on the entire balance and potentially an early withdrawal penalty if the retiree is under 59½.

This decision is not automatic, and the default choice is rarely the optimal one. Consult a qualified tax or financial professional before making any rollover or distribution decision. For a deeper look at how these choices interact with other income sources, see our guide to [pension and distribution decisions](/pension-distribution-decisions/).

### Deferred Compensation Decisions

Farmers Insurance employees and agents who participated in a deferred compensation program face a separate layer of decisions at retirement. Deferred compensation operates under different rules than a standard 401(k): distribution elections are typically set before retirement — sometimes years in advance — and the distributions are taxed as ordinary income in the year received.

Two questions matter most here. First, are the existing elections still aligned with the retiree's income plan? Elections set three or five years ago may not reflect the retiree's current situation. Second, in which years will deferred compensation distributions land — and what other income will they coincide with? Taking deferred compensation distributions in the same year as large 401(k) withdrawals or Social Security income can push combined income into a higher bracket. Consult a qualified tax professional on the timing and sequencing of deferred compensation distributions before retiring.

### Retiree Health Coverage Transition

Farmers Insurance employees leaving employment face a time-sensitive decision about retiree health coverage. Enrollment windows may be defined by the employer, and missing them can limit options significantly. For employees retiring before age 65, there is the added complexity of covering the period before Medicare eligibility begins.

Options in that bridge period may include COBRA continuation coverage, a retiree-specific plan if available, marketplace coverage, or a spouse's employer plan. Each option has different costs and coverage levels. At 65, Medicare enrollment begins — with its own set of decisions about Part A, Part B, supplemental coverage (Medigap), and Part D prescription coverage. Confirm enrollment deadlines with HR before your last day.

### Social Security Timing

For Farmers Insurance employees and agents who have accumulated Social Security credits over their careers, timing the start of benefits is one of the most consequential decisions in retirement planning — and one of the most permanent.

Claiming at 62 locks in a permanently reduced benefit. Waiting until 70 locks in the maximum monthly benefit. The break-even point — the age at which delayed claiming pays off in cumulative terms — is typically in the late 70s or early 80s. For people in good health with other income sources, delaying is often worth modeling. For people who need income sooner, claiming earlier may be worth considering.

The interaction between deferred compensation distributions, 401(k) withdrawals, and Social Security income affects taxable income every year those sources overlap. Getting the sequencing right — not just the claiming date — is part of Social Security planning. For more on how these income sources interact, see our guide to [retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/).

### How Farmers Insurance Agents and Producers Plan for Retirement Differently Than Corporate Employees

Independent Farmers agents and producers face a retirement transition that is structurally different from corporate employees. Production income is variable and does not simply stop — it winds down as the agent reduces activity, transitions clients, or reaches agreement on agency disposition. That wind-down period requires specific planning because the shift from variable production income to fixed retirement income is not automatic.

For agents, retirement income planning typically involves multiple sources that come online at different times: any applicable deferred compensation, IRA or other savings assets, agency-related considerations, and Social Security. The sequencing of those sources — and the tax picture in each year — requires planning that accounts for the variability of the wind-down period.

Agents who have built significant agency value over a career may also face decisions about how that value is recognized at or near retirement. These decisions can have meaningful tax implications. This guide frames these as decision categories only — the specifics depend on individual contracts, agreements, and circumstances. Consult a qualified financial and tax professional on any agency-related retirement considerations.

### Roth Conversion Window

For both corporate employees and agents, the years between retirement and age 73 — when required minimum distributions (RMDs) begin — may represent a window for Roth conversions. If deferred compensation distributions create income peaks in some years and valleys in others, the lower-income years may allow for Roth conversions at a lower effective tax rate than will be available once RMDs begin.

A Roth conversion involves moving pre-tax assets into a Roth IRA and paying ordinary income tax on the converted amount in the year of conversion. Converted assets then grow tax-free and are not subject to future RMDs. Whether this makes sense depends on account balances, current and future tax brackets, and the retiree's overall income picture. Consult a qualified tax professional before pursuing any Roth conversion. See also [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/).

## Questions Worth Asking Before You Retire From Farmers Insurance

These are not questions this guide can answer for you — they are questions to bring to a qualified financial and tax professional before your last day.

1. Have I reviewed my deferred compensation distribution elections — and do they still align with my current retirement income plan?
2. Do I understand the tax implications of taking 401(k) and deferred compensation distributions in the same year?
3. Have I confirmed my retiree health enrollment window, and do I know what options are available if I retire before age 65?
4. Have I thought through when I plan to claim Social Security and how that decision interacts with my other income sources?
5. If I am an agent, do I have a plan for the wind-down of production income — and how does that transition period affect my retirement income plan?
6. Do I have a withdrawal strategy for my retirement accounts — not just a rollover decision, but a plan for how and when I will draw on those assets?
7. Have I evaluated whether there is a Roth conversion opportunity in early retirement before RMDs begin at 73?
8. Do I know what my actual monthly income will look like in the first 12 months of retirement, from all sources combined?

## Retirement Income Considerations

Farmers Insurance retirees — whether corporate employees or agents — typically manage retirement income from multiple sources that operate on different timelines. 401(k) or IRA withdrawals are flexible within RMD rules. Deferred compensation distributions follow a schedule. Social Security has a claiming date. For agents, the income picture may include a wind-down period where production income is still present but declining.

The tax picture in each year depends on which sources are active and how much each contributes. High combined income in any single year can push income into a higher bracket, increase the taxable portion of Social Security benefits, and trigger IRMAA surcharges on Medicare premiums in subsequent years. Consult a qualified tax professional before finalizing any distribution strategy.

## Roth Conversion Considerations

The years between retirement and age 73 represent a window that is sometimes underused by Farmers Insurance retirees. If deferred compensation distributions are structured to arrive over multiple years — some higher-income, some lower — the lower-income years may allow for meaningful Roth conversions at a lower effective rate than will be available later.

For agents whose income is declining during a wind-down period, those transition years may also present Roth conversion opportunities before Social Security and RMDs add to the income floor in later years.

Roth assets grow tax-free and are not subject to RMDs, which makes them a flexible reserve in late retirement. Whether a conversion makes sense depends on the retiree's current tax bracket, expected future bracket, and account balances. This is not a decision to make without professional guidance. See our guide to [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/) for a framework, and consult a qualified tax professional before taking any action.

## Common Mistakes Farmers Insurance Employees and Agents Make at Retirement

**Not reviewing deferred compensation elections before retirement.** This is the most common pre-retirement oversight. Distribution elections made years ago may not reflect the retiree's current income plan. Review all existing elections — and confirm whether any changes are still possible — before your last day.

**Rolling 401(k) without a withdrawal strategy in place.** Completing a rollover to an IRA is a transaction, not a retirement income plan. Many Farmers Insurance retirees transfer assets to an IRA and then leave them without a structured withdrawal strategy. Know how and when you plan to draw on those assets before you complete the rollover.

**Missing the retiree health enrollment window.** This window can close quickly and the consequences can persist. Confirm your enrollment options and deadlines with HR before you leave — not after.

**Taking 401(k) and deferred compensation distributions in the same year without tax modeling.** Drawing from both sources in the same year can significantly increase taxable income. In some cases, it also triggers IRMAA surcharges on Medicare premiums for two subsequent years. Tax sequencing across retirement accounts and deferred compensation matters — consult a qualified tax professional before taking large distributions.

**For agents: not planning the wind-down.** Retiring as an agent is not a single date — it is a transition. Agents who do not plan the production wind-down in advance often face a disorganized income picture in the first years of retirement, with variable income declining unpredictably while fixed retirement income has not yet been structured. For more on [retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/), see our dedicated resource page.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What should Farmers Insurance employees do with their 401(k) when they retire?

The primary options are to leave assets in the plan, roll to an IRA, or take a distribution. Rolling to an IRA typically offers the broadest investment options and the most flexibility for managing withdrawals in retirement. Staying in the plan may make sense if the plan offers strong investment options at low cost, or if the retiree has specific needs such as accessing assets before age 59½ without penalty. Taking a full distribution triggers ordinary income tax on the entire balance and is generally the least advantageous option from a tax standpoint. Consult a qualified tax or financial professional before making any rollover decision.

### How does deferred compensation work at retirement for Farmers Insurance employees?

Deferred compensation distributions are governed by elections typically made before retirement — sometimes years in advance. The distributions are taxed as ordinary income in the year received. At retirement, it is worth confirming what the existing elections are and whether they still align with the retiree's income plan. In some cases, changes to distribution schedules may still be possible before retirement, subject to IRS rules. Consult a qualified tax professional on the timing, sequencing, and tax implications of deferred compensation distributions before finalizing any retirement plan.

### What are the retiree health insurance options Farmers Insurance employees need to plan for?

Employees retiring before age 65 need to understand what retiree coverage — if any — is available through the employer, and what it costs. COBRA continuation coverage and marketplace options are available alternatives during the bridge to Medicare. At 65, Medicare enrollment decisions begin, including choices about supplemental (Medigap) coverage and Part D. Enrollment windows at retirement can be time-sensitive. Confirm the specifics of your enrollment options with HR before your last day to avoid coverage gaps or lost enrollment opportunities.

### When is the right time to claim Social Security if you have deferred compensation income?

There is no single right answer — it depends on the retiree's health, other income sources, and how Social Security income interacts with deferred compensation distributions and 401(k) withdrawals. In years when deferred compensation distributions are high, adding Social Security income can push combined income into a higher bracket. In lower-income years, Social Security may be more advantageous to begin. Delaying Social Security to age 70 increases the monthly benefit permanently, which is often worthwhile for people in good health with other income sources. Consult a qualified financial professional to model the interaction of all income sources before choosing a claiming date.

### Are retirement decisions different for Farmers Insurance agents vs. corporate employees?

Yes — significantly. Corporate employees typically have a 401(k) or defined contribution plan, deferred compensation if eligible, and a standard retiree benefits transition. Independent agents and producers may have a different mix of savings vehicles, variable income that winds down rather than ending on a fixed date, and agency-related considerations that do not apply to corporate employees. Both groups face deferred compensation and Social Security timing decisions, but the overall retirement income picture and the transition structure are different enough that they require separate planning frameworks. This guide addresses both audiences — but the right plan depends on which category applies to you.

### Can Farmers Insurance retirees do a Roth conversion after they leave the company?

Yes — Roth conversions are available to anyone with pre-tax retirement assets. For Farmers Insurance retirees who leave before age 73, there may be a window between retirement and the start of required minimum distributions when taxable income is lower than it will be later. If deferred compensation distributions create income variability in early retirement, the lower-income years in that range may allow for Roth conversions at a lower effective rate. Whether this is worthwhile depends on the retiree's current tax bracket, account balances, and expected future income. Consult a qualified tax professional before pursuing any Roth conversion. See our [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/) guide for more.

### What is the biggest retirement mistake Farmers Insurance employees make?

Not reviewing deferred compensation elections before retirement is the most common and most avoidable mistake. Distribution elections set years earlier may not align with the retiree's current income plan — and in some cases, the window to change those elections closes before retirement. A close second is completing a 401(k) rollover without a withdrawal strategy in place. The rollover is a transaction; a retirement income plan is what tells you how to use those assets. For more on [retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/), see our dedicated guide.

### How do I wind down production income as a Farmers Insurance agent approaching retirement?

There is no single path — it depends on the agent's contract, book of business, and individual circumstances. What matters from a retirement planning standpoint is that the wind-down period is planned in advance, not managed reactively. Production income declining unpredictably while fixed retirement income is not yet structured creates an uncertain gap. Agents approaching retirement benefit from modeling what their income will look like in the wind-down years and the first years of full retirement — and structuring 401(k) or IRA withdrawals, deferred compensation distributions, and Social Security timing around that picture. Consult a qualified financial professional with experience working with self-employed individuals and agents.
