## Direct Answer

Government employees — particularly federal employees under FERS — often retire with three distinct income sources: a pension, Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) savings, and Social Security. Each has its own timing decisions, tax treatment, and interaction with the others. The decisions made around TSP distribution, the FERS survivor benefit election, and the Social Security filing date are among the most consequential — and most permanent — a government employee will make at retirement.

## Retirement Transition Overview

Government employment — whether at the federal, state, or local level — typically creates a layered retirement income structure. For federal employees under the Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS), that structure is a three-part system: a defined benefit pension, the Thrift Savings Plan (TSP), and Social Security.

This combination is often described as a secure retirement. In some ways it is — the pension provides a guaranteed income foundation, and the TSP and Social Security add supplemental layers. But layered income structures bring layered decisions. Each component has its own election timing, its own tax treatment, and its own interactions with the others.

State and local government employees face a parallel structure. The specific plan design varies — pension formulas, supplemental savings vehicles, and Social Security participation differ by employer and state — but the core decision landscape is similar: a pension base, a savings plan, and potentially Social Security, each requiring its own evaluation at retirement.

This guide covers the key decisions that government employees commonly face at the transition point. It does not describe specific plan benefits or provide individualized planning advice. For a deep dive into the TSP specifically, see our [TSP retirement guide](/tsp-retirement-guide/).

## Retirement Decisions Government Employees Often Face

### TSP Distribution Decision

The Thrift Savings Plan gives federal employees several options at retirement — and rolling to an IRA is only one of them. Federal employees can leave the TSP balance invested and take withdrawals over time, elect installment payments (monthly, quarterly, or annually), purchase a TSP annuity, roll the balance to an IRA or eligible employer plan, or some combination of these.

Each option has different implications for investment control, fee structure, flexibility, and tax treatment. The TSP's G Fund — a unique option that earns government securities-level interest without principal loss — is not available in an IRA. Employees who roll out of the TSP give up access to that option permanently.

At the same time, an IRA may offer greater investment flexibility, estate planning features, and Roth conversion capability. The decision is worth evaluating on its own merits — not defaulting to a rollover because it seems like the standard move.

For more detail on TSP distribution options, see our [TSP retirement guide](/tsp-retirement-guide/). For broader income planning context, see [retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/).

### FERS Pension Election

The FERS pension provides a monthly income benefit based on years of federal service and high-3 average salary. At retirement, federal employees elect how to receive that benefit — and the most significant sub-decision within the pension election is whether to elect the survivor benefit.

The FERS pension election is not a casual form. It is typically irrevocable once processed. Understanding what has been committed to — and what has been waived — before submitting the election is essential. See [pension distribution options](/pension-distribution-decisions/) for context on how pension elections are typically structured.

### Survivor Benefit Election

The FERS survivor benefit election determines whether a surviving spouse receives a portion of the retiree's pension after the retiree's death. Electing the survivor benefit reduces the retiree's monthly pension payment. Waiving it increases the retiree's monthly income but eliminates the spouse's protection permanently.

This is a household income decision — not just a line item on a retirement form. The right answer depends on the spouse's own income sources, health, age difference between spouses, and the household's financial resilience if the retiree dies first. Waiving the survivor benefit is a legitimate choice in some households; it deserves full analysis, not a quick decision under deadline pressure.

### Social Security Timing

Most FERS employees are also eligible for Social Security, which adds a third timing decision to retirement planning. Social Security can be claimed as early as age 62 (at a permanently reduced benefit) or delayed up to age 70 (at an increased benefit). The differential between the earliest and latest filing dates is substantial.

For FERS retirees, the interaction between pension income, TSP distributions, and Social Security affects how much of Social Security is taxable, whether Medicare premium surcharges (IRMAA) apply in a given year, and whether early retirement years create space for Roth conversion. Consult a tax professional regarding tax sequencing specific to your situation.

### How the WEP and GPO Repeal Affects Government Employee Social Security Benefits

The Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) and the Government Pension Offset (GPO) were federal provisions that reduced Social Security benefits for many government employees. WEP reduced benefits for workers who received a pension from non-Social Security-covered employment and also worked in Social Security-covered jobs. GPO reduced spousal or survivor Social Security benefits for individuals who received a government pension from non-covered employment.

Both provisions were eliminated by the Social Security Fairness Act, signed into law in January 2025. The repeal is retroactive to benefits payable for December 2023 onward. Government employees and retirees who previously had their Social Security benefits reduced under WEP or GPO should contact the Social Security Administration to confirm whether their benefit amount has been or will be adjusted. For some individuals, this may result in meaningfully increased or restored monthly benefits.

### State and Local Government Employees

State and local government employees face a parallel decision landscape, though the specific plan structures vary significantly by state, employer, and bargaining unit. Common elements include a defined benefit pension, a supplemental savings vehicle (often a 457(b) or 403(b) plan), and in some cases Social Security (participation varies by state and employer).

State and local employees should pay particular attention to whether their plan coordinates with Social Security, whether the WEP or GPO repeal may entitle them to increased or restored Social Security benefits, and what distribution options exist for their supplemental savings plan at retirement. If you are a public sector employee in California, many plans have specific rules worth understanding before your retirement date. Contact your HR or benefits office to confirm plan-specific details.

If you are in the private sector — for example, working in aerospace — see our [Boeing retirement transition decisions](/boeing-retirement-transition-guide/) guide for relevant context.

## Questions Worth Asking Before You Retire From Government Service

These questions are designed to surface gaps before they become permanent decisions made without full information.

1. Have I confirmed whether the WEP or GPO previously reduced my Social Security benefit — and whether the Social Security Fairness Act entitles me to an increased or restored benefit?
2. Do I understand what my FERS survivor benefit election means for my spouse's income if I die first?
3. Have I evaluated the TSP distribution options before defaulting to an IRA rollover?
4. If I leave the TSP, do I understand that access to the G Fund ends permanently?
5. What is my income picture in the window between my retirement date and when I plan to claim Social Security?
6. Have I confirmed the submission deadline for my FERS survivor benefit election and pension election?
7. Do I know how my pension income will affect the taxability of my Social Security benefits?
8. Have I identified whether the gap between retirement and age 73 creates a Roth conversion opportunity for my TSP balance?

## Retirement Income Considerations

Federal employees who retire with a FERS pension, TSP balance, and Social Security eligibility are managing three distinct income streams — each with different tax treatment, timing flexibility, and interaction effects.

FERS pension income is taxed as ordinary income. TSP withdrawals (from traditional TSP contributions) are also taxed as ordinary income. Social Security benefits may be partially taxable depending on combined income. Activating all three simultaneously can push income into higher tax brackets and trigger Medicare premium surcharges. Consult a tax professional regarding IRMAA implications specific to your situation.

The window between retirement and the onset of required minimum distributions at age 73 is often a period of relative income flexibility. How the pension, TSP, and Social Security are sequenced during that window shapes the tax picture for the full retirement.

For a broader look at how these income layers interact, see [retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/).

## Roth Conversion Considerations

Federal employees with traditional TSP balances — and traditional IRA assets — face required minimum distributions beginning at age 73. Roth TSP accounts are also currently subject to RMDs (unlike Roth IRAs, which have no lifetime RMD requirement for the original owner). Rolling Roth TSP assets to a Roth IRA before RMDs begin eliminates that distribution requirement for those assets.

For FERS retirees who retire in their late 50s or early 60s, the window before age 73 may create years of lower taxable income — particularly if Social Security is being delayed and TSP withdrawals are managed carefully. That window may be an opportunity to convert pre-tax TSP or IRA balances to Roth, spreading the tax cost over multiple years at potentially lower rates.

Whether a Roth conversion is appropriate depends on current vs. projected future tax rates, other income sources, state tax treatment, and individual circumstances. Consult a tax professional before making Roth conversion decisions. For more context, see [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/).

## Common Mistakes Government Employees Make at Retirement

Understanding where things go wrong is part of making a better decision. For a full list, see [retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/).

**1. Rolling the TSP to an IRA without evaluating the alternatives.**
The TSP rollover is often treated as automatic. It is not. The G Fund, the TSP's low-cost structure, and TSP-specific distribution options may be worth preserving depending on individual circumstances. Evaluate first; decide second.

**2. Selecting the TSP annuity without comparing it to the private annuity market.**
The TSP does offer an annuity option. It is not necessarily the most competitive option available. Government employees who want guaranteed income from their TSP balance are encouraged to compare TSP annuity rates to private annuity alternatives before making an irrevocable election.

**3. Waiving the FERS survivor benefit without full household income analysis.**
Waiving the survivor benefit increases the retiree's monthly income. It also eliminates the spouse's protection permanently. This decision is frequently made too quickly — treated as a cost reduction rather than a household income structure decision.

**4. Not checking whether the WEP or GPO repeal entitles you to increased Social Security benefits.**
The Social Security Fairness Act eliminated both provisions in January 2025. Government employees and retirees who previously had benefits reduced should contact the Social Security Administration to confirm whether their benefit amount has been or will be adjusted.

**5. Treating the FERS pension election as a formality.**
The pension election — including the survivor benefit decision — is typically irrevocable once submitted. It deserves the same level of analysis as any major financial decision, not a quick review of a form the week before retirement.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should federal employees roll their TSP to an IRA when they retire?

Not necessarily. Rolling the TSP to an IRA is one option — not the default. The TSP offers its own distribution options (installment payments, partial withdrawals, and more), a low-cost investment structure, and access to the G Fund, which is not available in an IRA. Employees who roll out of the TSP give up those features permanently. At the same time, an IRA may offer greater investment flexibility and estate planning options. The right choice depends on individual circumstances. For a deeper look, see our [TSP retirement guide](/tsp-retirement-guide/).

### What is the FERS survivor benefit election and do I have to take it?

The FERS survivor benefit election allows you to designate a portion of your monthly pension to continue to a surviving spouse after your death. Electing it reduces your monthly payment; waiving it eliminates the spousal protection permanently. You are not required to elect the maximum survivor benefit — partial elections are available — but any election is typically irrevocable once submitted. This decision deserves a full household income analysis, ideally before the retirement paperwork deadline.

### Were the WEP and GPO eliminated — and what does that mean for government employees' Social Security benefits?

Yes. The Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP) and the Government Pension Offset (GPO) were both eliminated by the Social Security Fairness Act, signed into law in January 2025. WEP had reduced Social Security benefits for workers who received a pension from non-Social Security-covered employment and also worked in Social Security-covered jobs. GPO had reduced spousal or survivor Social Security benefits for those receiving a government pension from non-covered employment.

The repeal is retroactive to benefits payable for December 2023 onward. Government employees and retirees who previously had their Social Security benefits reduced under WEP or GPO should contact the Social Security Administration directly to confirm whether their benefit amount has been or will be adjusted. For some individuals, this may result in meaningfully increased or restored monthly payments.

### Can I leave my TSP invested after I retire from federal service?

Yes. Federal employees can leave their TSP balance in the plan after retirement, subject to plan rules. The TSP remains available for investment and distributions. The requirement to take required minimum distributions begins at age 73, at which point TSP distributions are subject to RMD rules just like an IRA. Leaving the TSP in place maintains access to the G Fund and the TSP's low-cost fund options — which may be worth preserving depending on individual circumstances.

### What is the difference between the TSP annuity and TSP installment payments?

TSP installment payments allow you to take regular distributions (monthly, quarterly, or annually) from your TSP balance while keeping the remaining balance invested. The balance fluctuates with market performance, and you retain control over the account. A TSP annuity converts the TSP balance into a guaranteed income stream for life (or a defined period), similar to purchasing an annuity from an insurance company — which it essentially is. The annuity is irrevocable and removes control of the underlying balance. Each approach has different implications for flexibility, longevity risk, and estate planning.

### How does FERS pension income affect when I should claim Social Security?

FERS pension income is counted as ordinary income. When Social Security is added on top, combined income can increase the percentage of Social Security benefits subject to tax and may trigger IRMAA Medicare premium surcharges. For FERS retirees with substantial pension income, delaying Social Security — and using the early retirement years for strategic TSP distributions or Roth conversions — may reduce long-term tax exposure. This is a sequencing question that depends on individual circumstances. Consult a tax professional regarding tax strategy specific to your situation.

### Do state and local government employees have the same retirement decisions as federal employees?

The categories of decisions are similar — pension election, supplemental savings plan disposition, Social Security timing — but the specific plan structures differ. State and local plans vary significantly by employer, state, and employee classification. Social Security participation is not universal for state and local employees; some participate fully, some partially, and some not at all. Given the repeal of WEP and GPO under the Social Security Fairness Act in January 2025, state and local government employees who previously had Social Security benefits reduced should confirm with the SSA whether their benefit has been or will be adjusted. State and local employees should also confirm their plan-specific rules with HR or a benefits office and work with an advisor familiar with their jurisdiction's plan structure.

### What happens to my Roth TSP when I retire — is it treated the same as a Roth IRA?

No. Roth TSP accounts are subject to required minimum distributions beginning at age 73, unlike Roth IRAs (which have no lifetime RMD requirement for the original owner). Federal employees who want to eliminate RMDs on Roth assets can roll their Roth TSP balance to a Roth IRA before RMDs begin — which removes the distribution requirement for those assets going forward. The rollover itself is generally not a taxable event, but the timing and strategy around it matters. Consult a tax professional regarding your specific situation.
