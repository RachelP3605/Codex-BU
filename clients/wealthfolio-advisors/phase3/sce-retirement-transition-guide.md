## Retirement Transition Overview

Southern California Edison (SCE), a subsidiary of Edison International, is one of the largest electric utilities in the western United States and one of the largest employers in Southern California. Employees who have spent long careers at SCE — in operations, engineering, transmission and distribution, or corporate functions — often arrive at retirement with a combination of retirement assets that interact in ways that take planning to manage well.

For many SCE employees, retirement is not a single financial event. It is a sequence of enrollment decisions, elections, and choices that compound over time. The stakes are higher in Southern California than in many other regions. Healthcare costs, housing costs, and the general cost of living in the greater Los Angeles and Long Beach areas mean that income sufficiency in retirement is a real planning concern — not a theoretical one.

This guide is not a summary of SCE's benefits program. It is a decision education resource for employees who are beginning to think about what comes next.

## Retirement Decisions SCE Employees Often Face

### Pension Distribution Election: Lump Sum vs. Monthly Income

For SCE employees eligible for a defined benefit pension, the distribution election is one of the most consequential decisions in retirement planning — and one of the most permanent. Once made, the choice between a lump sum and a lifetime monthly payment is generally irrevocable.

The decision involves more than comparing two numbers. Monthly income provides predictability and longevity protection. A lump sum introduces flexibility — and responsibility. The right answer depends on health, other income sources, a spouse's situation, and the retiree's comfort managing assets over a long retirement. For more on how to evaluate this decision, see our guide to [pension distribution options](/pension-distribution-decisions/).

### Deferred Savings Plan Decisions

SCE employees who have accumulated assets in a deferred savings plan face a different set of decisions at retirement: whether to leave assets in the plan, roll them to an IRA, or take distributions. Each path has different tax implications, investment options, and flexibility.

Rolling to an IRA is often the most flexible option — but "flexible" is not automatically better. The right choice depends on the retiree's income needs in early retirement, their tax situation, and whether the plan's investment options and cost structure make staying in the plan worth considering. Consult a qualified tax or financial professional before making any rollover decision.

### Deferred Compensation Decisions

SCE employees who participated in a deferred compensation program face a separate layer of decisions at retirement. Deferred compensation is different from a standard savings plan — it operates under different tax rules, and the distribution timing elections are often set before retirement, not at the time of distribution.

If distribution elections were set years ago, it is worth reviewing whether those elections still align with your retirement income plan. Deferred compensation distributions are taxed as ordinary income in the year received, which means the year and sequence in which you take those distributions can significantly affect your tax picture. Consult a qualified tax professional on deferred compensation distribution planning, particularly if you are also receiving pension income in the same years.

### Survivor Benefit Election

For married SCE employees, the pension survivor benefit election is often considered at the same time as the distribution election — but it deserves its own attention. Choosing a higher monthly payment for yourself often means reduced or no income to a surviving spouse. Choosing a survivor benefit reduces your monthly payment in exchange for continued income to your spouse after your death.

This is not a decision to make under deadline pressure. It involves thinking through your spouse's financial position, other income sources, life expectancy considerations, and whether life insurance or other assets provide alternative survivor income. There is no universally correct answer.

### Retiree Medical Coverage Transition

For many SCE employees, retiree medical coverage represents one of the most time-sensitive decisions at retirement. Enrollment windows may be defined by the employer, and missing them can limit options in ways that are difficult or impossible to correct later.

Employees retiring before age 65 face the added complexity of bridging to Medicare. Understanding what coverage options are available in that gap period — and what they cost — is an important part of pre-retirement planning. Once you reach 65, Medicare enrollment has its own timeline and decisions, including Medicare Supplement and Part D coverage choices.

### Social Security Timing Relative to Pension Income

SCE employees with a pension often have more flexibility in Social Security timing than retirees who depend immediately on Social Security income. But that flexibility has limits — delaying Social Security to age 70 increases lifetime benefit, while claiming at 62 permanently reduces it.

The interaction between pension income, deferred savings distributions, and Social Security benefits affects the overall tax picture. Taking large distributions from deferred accounts in years when you are also receiving pension income and Social Security can push income into higher tax brackets. Getting the sequencing right matters. See our resource on [retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/) for more on how these income sources interact.

## Questions Worth Asking Before You Retire From SCE

These are not questions this guide can answer for you — they are questions to bring to a qualified financial and tax professional before your retirement date.

1. Have I confirmed my pension election deadline, and do I have enough time to evaluate the options carefully before it closes?
2. If I am married, have my spouse and I discussed the survivor benefit election — and what it means for both of us?
3. Do I know when my deferred compensation distributions are scheduled to begin, and do those elections still align with my current retirement income plan?
4. Do I understand how my deferred compensation, pension, and Social Security income will interact in the same tax year?
5. Have I confirmed my retiree medical enrollment window, and do I know what coverage options are available if I retire before age 65?
6. Have I thought through when I plan to claim Social Security and how that timing affects my overall income and tax picture?
7. Do I have a withdrawal plan for my deferred savings — not just a rollover decision, but a plan for how and when I will draw on those assets?
8. Is Southern California still where I plan to live in retirement, and have I factored local costs into my income projections?

## Retirement Income Considerations

SCE employees who retire with a pension, deferred savings, and deferred compensation are managing multiple income streams that do not all behave the same way. Pension income is fixed and begins at retirement. Deferred compensation distributions follow a schedule. Deferred savings withdrawals are flexible — within the rules for RMDs beginning at age 73.

The sequencing of these income streams affects taxes every year. Drawing heavily from deferred accounts in years when pension income is already substantial can increase taxable income significantly. Social Security benefits may be partially taxable depending on total income. High income in any given year can trigger higher Medicare premiums (IRMAA) in subsequent years. Consult a qualified tax professional before finalizing any distribution strategy.

Southern California's cost of living is a real variable in this planning. Retirees in the greater Los Angeles, Long Beach, and Orange County areas face housing costs, healthcare costs, and general expenses that can make income gaps visible quickly. Building a retirement income plan that accounts for actual local expenses — not national averages — gives SCE retirees a more honest picture of what they need. ## Roth Conversion Considerations

For SCE employees who retire before age 73 — and many do — there is often a window between the end of employment income and the start of required minimum distributions (RMDs) when taxable income is lower than it will be later. That window is sometimes worth examining as a potential Roth conversion opportunity.

A Roth conversion involves moving pre-tax dollars from a traditional IRA or deferred savings account into a Roth IRA, paying ordinary income tax on the converted amount in the year of conversion. Converted assets then grow tax-free and are not subject to RMDs.

Whether a Roth conversion makes sense depends on how much pension income an SCE employee receives, how large their deferred savings balance is, and what their tax bracket looks like in early retirement versus later retirement. For SCE employees with meaningful pension income, the conversion window may be narrower than for retirees with no fixed income floor.

Consult a qualified tax professional before pursuing any Roth conversion strategy. See also our guide to [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/).

## Common Mistakes SCE Employees Make at Retirement

**Making the pension election under deadline pressure.** The pension distribution election is permanent. Choosing between a lump sum and a monthly payment — or between pension options — requires time to evaluate. Employees who wait until the last few weeks before retirement to engage with this decision often do so without enough time to model it properly.

**Not reviewing deferred compensation elections before retirement.** Deferred compensation distribution schedules are often set years before retirement. Many employees arrive at retirement without having reviewed whether those elections still match their income plan. Review the elections well before your last day.

**Missing the retiree medical enrollment window.** This is a time-sensitive decision. SCE retirees who do not enroll in available retiree medical coverage during the enrollment window may find their options are significantly limited afterward.

**Taking large deferred account distributions without tax modeling.** Drawing heavily from deferred savings in the same year you begin receiving pension income — without modeling the combined tax impact — can push income into a higher bracket and trigger IRMAA surcharges on Medicare premiums in subsequent years. Consult a qualified tax professional before taking large distributions.

**Treating rollover as the last step.** Rolling a deferred savings balance to an IRA is a decision — but it is not the end of the plan. Many SCE retirees complete the rollover without a withdrawal strategy in place. An IRA without a distribution plan is not a retirement income plan. For more on [retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/), see our dedicated resource page.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should SCE employees take a pension lump sum or monthly income at retirement?

There is no universal answer — this decision depends on health, age, other income sources, spouse situation, and the retiree's comfort level managing a lump sum over a long retirement. Monthly income provides predictability and longevity protection. A lump sum gives flexibility but shifts investment and longevity risk to the retiree. The key is that this decision is typically irrevocable, so it deserves careful analysis before any election is made. A qualified financial professional can help model both options against your specific retirement income plan. See our guide to [pension distribution options](/pension-distribution-decisions/) for a framework.

### What should SCE employees do with their deferred savings plan when they retire?

The main options are generally to leave assets in the plan, roll to an IRA, or take a distribution. Rolling to an IRA is often the most flexible path, but the right answer depends on the plan's investment options and fees, the retiree's income needs, and the tax picture for the year of the rollover. A partial rollover is also possible in some cases. This is not a decision to make by default — it is worth evaluating with a qualified tax or financial professional before taking action.

### When is the right time to retire from Southern California Edison?

Retirement timing at SCE depends on several factors that are specific to each employee: pension eligibility and how benefits change with additional years of service, deferred savings balances, retiree medical eligibility, and personal income needs. There is no single right answer, but the planning window matters — decisions about when to retire should ideally begin at least 12 to 24 months before the target date, not in the final weeks of employment.

### How does SCE pension income affect Social Security filing decisions?

Pension income does not directly reduce Social Security benefits for most SCE employees — but it does affect the tax picture around Social Security. When pension income and Social Security are received in the same years, a higher percentage of Social Security benefits may be taxable. This interplay is one reason why the timing of Social Security filing and deferred savings withdrawals is worth modeling together, not in isolation. Consult a qualified tax professional on how your specific income mix will interact.

### What are the retiree medical options SCE employees need to plan for?

SCE employees retiring before age 65 need to understand what retiree medical coverage is available through the employer and what it costs, as well as marketplace options for the bridge period to Medicare. At 65, Medicare enrollment decisions begin — including choices about supplemental coverage and Part D. Enrollment windows can be time-sensitive, and missing them can create coverage gaps or long-term limitations. Confirm your specific enrollment deadlines well before retirement.

### What is the biggest retirement mistake SCE employees make?

The most common and consequential mistake is making the pension election — a permanent, irrevocable choice — without adequate time or information. Employees who begin the planning process close to their retirement date often do not have enough time to properly evaluate the lump sum versus monthly income decision, model the survivor benefit options, or review deferred compensation elections before their last day. Starting the planning process 12 to 24 months before retirement significantly reduces this risk.

### Can SCE employees do a Roth conversion after they retire?

Yes — Roth conversions are available to anyone with pre-tax retirement savings and earned or converted income. For SCE employees who retire before age 73, there may be a window when pension income is the primary income source and deferred savings withdrawals have not yet begun. In some situations, that window allows for Roth conversions at a lower effective tax rate than will be available once RMDs begin at 73. Whether this is worthwhile depends on the retiree's pension income level, account balances, and expected future tax bracket. Consult a qualified tax professional before pursuing any Roth conversion. See our [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/) guide for more.

### How does deferred compensation affect retirement income taxes for SCE retirees?

Deferred compensation distributions are taxed as ordinary income in the year received. If those distributions coincide with pension income, Social Security, or large IRA withdrawals in the same year, the combined effect can push income into a higher bracket and potentially trigger IRMAA Medicare premium surcharges in subsequent years. The sequencing and timing of deferred compensation distributions — relative to other income sources — is a meaningful tax planning variable. Consult a qualified tax professional on distribution timing before you retire.
