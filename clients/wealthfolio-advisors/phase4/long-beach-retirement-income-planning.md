## Retirement Income Sources: What Most Long Beach Residents Are Working With

Most Long Beach-area retirees are working with some combination of the following:

### Social Security

Social Security is the income source most people think about first and often understand the least. You can file as early as 62, but filing early permanently reduces your monthly benefit. Delaying past your full retirement age — up to age 70 — increases the benefit each year you wait. For most people, the right filing age is not the earliest available date. It depends on your health, your other income sources, your spouse's benefit situation, and how much income you need in the gap years before you file.

If you have a pension, the Social Security question changes: you may have less urgency to file early because you already have guaranteed income. Part-time or consulting work before full retirement age can temporarily reduce Social Security benefits if you file early — another reason to evaluate the timing carefully.

### IRA and 401(k) Withdrawals

Once your money is in a traditional IRA or 401(k), withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income at both the federal and California state level. The question is not just how much to withdraw — it is when to start and in what order relative to your other income. Withdrawing too much in a single year can push you into a higher tax bracket or trigger Medicare surcharges (IRMAA). Withdrawing too little before age 73 may mean missing an opportunity to manage your tax situation before Required Minimum Distributions create forced taxable income.

For background on the rollover decisions that often precede this question, see [what to do with your old 401(k)](/old-401k-decisions/) and [Long Beach retirement rollover guidance](/long-beach-retirement-rollover-guidance/).

### Pension Income

Long Beach has a substantial population of retirees with defined benefit pensions. Boeing's Seal Beach operations, Southern California Edison, the City of Long Beach, the Port, and Long Beach Unified School District are among the larger employers in the area with pension plans. If you have a pension, it changes your income planning in a meaningful way: you have a guaranteed base of income regardless of market performance. That changes how you think about the 401(k)/IRA, how aggressively you need to draw from it, and whether Social Security timing flexibility is more available to you.

Pension elections themselves — whether to take a lump sum or monthly benefit, and which survivor option to select — are separate decisions that compound the complexity. See [pension distribution options](/pension-distribution-decisions/) for more on that question.

### Roth IRA

A Roth IRA is funded with after-tax dollars. Qualified withdrawals in retirement are tax-free, and Roth accounts are not subject to Required Minimum Distributions during the owner's lifetime. If you have a Roth IRA, it typically makes sense to draw from it last — after taxable accounts and traditional IRAs — so it continues to grow tax-free as long as possible. If you do not have a Roth IRA now but have a traditional IRA, the years between retirement and age 73 may be a meaningful window to consider partial Roth conversions.

### Part-Time or Consulting Income

Many Long Beach residents continue working part-time or consulting after leaving their primary employer. This income is taxable and, depending on your age and when you have claimed Social Security, may affect your benefits. If you claim Social Security before your full retirement age and continue to earn income, the Social Security Administration may reduce your benefit temporarily. After full retirement age, earnings no longer reduce the benefit. Consult a financial professional to understand how part-time income interacts with Social Security in your situation.

See [retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/) for a broader look at how these sources are coordinated.

## Withdrawal Sequencing: Which Accounts to Draw From First

One of the most consequential and least understood elements of retirement income planning is the order in which you draw from your accounts.

The general logic that advisors often start with: draw from taxable accounts first, then traditional IRA and 401(k) accounts, then Roth accounts last. The reasoning is that taxable accounts have already been taxed; drawing them down first gives tax-deferred and tax-free accounts more time to grow. Drawing from traditional accounts in the middle years — before RMDs force the issue — can help manage your tax bracket. Preserving the Roth until last maximizes the time the tax-free money has to compound.

That said, this general sequence is not right for everyone. If you are in a low-income year early in retirement, it may make sense to pull from the traditional IRA rather than wait for RMDs to force larger distributions later. If you have a pension covering your core expenses, you may have flexibility to be more strategic with when and how much you draw from other accounts.

The point is not that there is one correct sequence — it is that the sequence is a decision, not a default. Making it deliberately, with your full income picture in view, tends to produce better outcomes than following the path of least resistance.

## Roth Conversion Considerations

The years between retirement and age 73, when Required Minimum Distributions begin, can represent a meaningful window for Roth conversion planning. If your income is lower in those years — because you have left your employer but have not yet started Social Security or RMDs — you may be in a lower tax bracket than you will be once RMDs arrive. Converting a portion of your traditional IRA to a Roth during this window adds to your taxable income in the conversion year, but it reduces the future RMD balance and creates tax-free income later.

This strategy is not appropriate for everyone. The conversion is a taxable event, and California taxes the converted amount as ordinary income. Whether the math works in your favor depends on your current bracket, your projected future income, and your timeline. Consult a qualified tax professional before pursuing Roth conversions. For more background, see [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/).

## Required Minimum Distributions

Beginning at age 73, the IRS requires that you take a minimum distribution each year from traditional IRA and 401(k) accounts. The amount is calculated based on your account balance and a life expectancy factor published by the IRS. You cannot skip RMDs — failing to take the required amount results in a significant penalty. Roth IRA accounts are not subject to RMDs during the owner's lifetime.

For many retirees, RMDs are larger than they expected because the account grew significantly during the working years. That forced income can push you into a higher tax bracket, increase the portion of Social Security that is taxable, or trigger Medicare IRMAA surcharges. The time to plan for RMDs is before they begin — not when the first letter from the IRS arrives. Consult a tax professional regarding RMD calculations and penalties specific to your situation.

## Common Retirement Income Planning Mistakes

### Drawing From the Wrong Account First

Defaulting to the most accessible account — usually the traditional IRA or 401(k) — without considering the tax implications can accelerate the tax bill unnecessarily. A Roth account left untouched continues to grow tax-free. A taxable account drawn down first preserves the tax-advantaged accounts longer.

### Claiming Social Security at 62 Without Evaluating the Lifetime Impact

Filing as early as possible feels like a safe choice — you get the money sooner. But permanently reduced monthly benefits can be costly over a long retirement. For most people, the filing decision deserves careful analysis, not a default answer. See [retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/).

### Ignoring California's Tax on Retirement Income

California taxes IRA withdrawals, pension distributions, and most retirement income at ordinary income rates. A withdrawal strategy that is tax-efficient federally may still carry a significant state tax bill. Planning for California's treatment of retirement distributions is not optional for Long Beach residents.

### Failing to Plan for RMDs Before They Arrive

Waiting until age 73 to think about RMDs means missing years of opportunity to reduce the traditional IRA balance through conversions or strategic withdrawals. Arriving at 73 with a large traditional IRA and a full Social Security benefit can mean a sudden jump in taxable income that is difficult to manage. These are among the most common [retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/).

### Treating the Rollover as the End of the Planning Process

A rollover moves money from a 401(k) to an IRA. That is one step — and an important one — but it is not an income strategy. Many people complete the rollover and then stop, leaving the money in cash or a default investment while assuming the hard decisions are behind them. The rollover is typically the beginning of the income planning work, not the conclusion.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I turn my 401(k) or IRA into retirement income in Long Beach?

The process starts with deciding how much income you need, what sources will cover it, and in what order you will draw from your accounts. For most people, this means coordinating Social Security timing, IRA or 401(k) withdrawals, and any pension income so that the combined cash flow covers expenses while minimizing taxes. There is no single formula — the right approach depends on your account balances, your age, your income needs, your tax situation, and what other guaranteed income you have. A structured review of these factors is a reasonable first step before making withdrawal decisions.

### When should I claim Social Security if I also have a pension?

Having a pension generally means you have guaranteed income that does not depend on when you file for Social Security. That can give you more flexibility to delay filing — which increases the monthly benefit permanently. However, the right answer depends on your specific pension amount, your health, your spouse's situation, and your other income needs. There is no universal rule. Consult a financial professional to model the different scenarios before deciding. The break-even calculation alone is not sufficient — the full income picture matters.

### Does California tax Social Security income?

No. California does not tax Social Security benefits, which is different from the federal treatment (where up to 85% of Social Security income may be taxable depending on your combined income). However, California does tax IRA withdrawals, 401(k) distributions, and most pension income as ordinary income. Retirement income planning in California involves understanding which sources are taxed at the state level and which are not — Social Security is one of the few that is not.

### What is the right order to withdraw from my retirement accounts?

The general starting framework is: taxable accounts first, then traditional IRA and 401(k), then Roth accounts last. This approach tends to be tax-efficient over time because it gives tax-deferred and tax-free accounts more time to grow. But the right sequence depends on your specific income sources, your tax bracket in different years, your RMD timeline, and whether there are Roth conversion opportunities in the gap years. The sequence is a decision — not a default. A qualified financial professional can help you model the tax implications of different approaches.

### How do required minimum distributions affect my retirement income plan?

RMDs begin at age 73 and require you to withdraw a minimum amount from traditional IRA and 401(k) accounts each year. That withdrawal is taxable income — whether you need the money or not. If your RMDs are large enough, they can increase the taxable portion of your Social Security benefits, push you into a higher tax bracket, or trigger Medicare IRMAA surcharges. Planning before age 73 — through Roth conversions, strategic early withdrawals, or other approaches — can reduce the impact. Failing to take a required distribution results in a significant IRS penalty. Consult a tax professional for specifics. Roth accounts are not subject to RMDs during the owner's lifetime.

### I have a pension from the City of Long Beach — do I still need to plan retirement income?

Yes. A pension provides a guaranteed base of income, which is genuinely valuable — but it does not eliminate the need for income planning. You still have decisions to make about when to draw from your IRA or 401(k), when to claim Social Security, and how to sequence withdrawals in a way that manages your California tax bill. In some cases, having a pension actually creates more complexity: the guaranteed income may push you into a higher bracket when combined with Social Security and RMDs, making the years before 73 more important for proactive planning. The pension is the foundation — the rest of the plan is built on top of it.

### What is a Roth conversion, and why do advisors recommend doing it before age 73?

A Roth conversion moves money from a traditional IRA to a Roth IRA. The converted amount is taxable income in the year of conversion — you pay taxes now; the money then grows tax-free and is not subject to RMDs. Advisors often suggest considering conversions before age 73 because income is typically lower in those years (after leaving work, before RMDs begin), which may mean a lower tax rate than when RMDs arrive. This strategy is not right for everyone, and California taxes the conversion as ordinary income. Consult a qualified tax professional before converting.

### How much retirement income do I need to retire in Southern California?

There is no universal answer — anyone who gives you a single number without knowing your situation is guessing. The amount depends on your housing situation, lifestyle expectations, healthcare costs, and what you want retirement to look like. Southern California's cost of living is higher than most of the country, and that is worth accounting for honestly. Rather than working from a generic rule of thumb, build an actual income picture from your specific accounts, expenses, and income sources.
