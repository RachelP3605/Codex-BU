## What Should You Do With Your 401(k) When You Leave a Job or Retire?

When you leave an employer in Long Beach — or retire — you typically have three options for your 401(k): leave it in the plan, roll it to an IRA, or roll it to a new employer's plan. There is no single right answer. The decision depends on your age, income needs, and what you plan to do with the money. A rollover is not a retirement plan — it is one decision within a larger plan.

---

## Key Takeaways

- A 401(k) rollover is not the same as a retirement income plan — rolling over the account creates an IRA; it does not create a strategy for how to use the money.
- You have options beyond rolling to an IRA: leaving the balance in the former employer's plan is sometimes the right call, especially if the plan has low-cost investment options or if you are between ages 55 and 59½.
- Direct rollovers (plan-to-plan or plan-to-IRA) avoid the 20% mandatory withholding that applies to indirect rollovers — the method matters.
- California taxes IRA withdrawals as ordinary income — the rollover itself is not a taxable event, but future distributions will be taxed by both the IRS and the California Franchise Tax Board.
- Rolling over before you have an income strategy often leads to the wrong decisions about when and how to withdraw — sequencing matters.
- Many Long Beach-area workers have both a pension and a 401(k) — the rollover decision should be evaluated alongside the [pension distribution options](/pension-distribution-decisions/), not in isolation.

## Your 401(k) Rollover Options

Understanding your options is the starting point. None of these choices is automatically correct. Each depends on your full financial picture.

### Option 1: Leave the Money in Your Former Employer's Plan

This option is often overlooked, but it is sometimes the right call. If your former employer's plan has institutional-class investment options with low expense ratios, the plan may be cheaper than an IRA you would open at a brokerage. If you are between ages 55 and 59½ and separated from service in or after the year you turned 55, you may be able to take penalty-free withdrawals from the employer plan — a flexibility that disappears if you roll to an IRA, where the 59½ rule applies. Leaving the money in the plan also keeps it under ERISA protections, which in some cases are stronger than those for IRAs.

The downside: you have no ongoing relationship with the former employer's plan administrator, investment options may be limited, and many plans eventually force distributions or complicate account access for former employees.

### Option 2: Roll to an IRA

This is the most common choice, and for good reason. An IRA typically offers broader investment options, more flexibility in beneficiary designations, and easier coordination with a financial advisor. The rollover itself is a tax-neutral event when done correctly — no taxes are due at the time of transfer, and the money continues to grow tax-deferred. Future withdrawals from a traditional IRA are taxed as ordinary income at both the federal and California state level.

To learn more about what happens after the rollover, see [what to do with your old 401(k)](/old-401k-decisions/).

### Option 3: Roll to a New Employer's Plan

If you are moving to a new job, you may have the option to roll your old 401(k) into your new employer's plan. This consolidates accounts and keeps the money under ERISA protection. The catch: not all plans accept incoming rollovers, and the new plan's investment options may or may not be competitive. This option is less common in the context of full retirement.

### Option 4: Cash Out

Taking the money as a distribution is always an option — but it is the one that most often costs people the most. A cash-out triggers immediate income taxes on the full balance, plus a 10% early withdrawal penalty if you are under 59½. California will also collect income tax on the distribution. For a large balance, this can mean losing 30% or more to taxes in a single year. If you are considering this option because you need cash now, it is worth exploring whether there are other sources first.

## Direct Rollover vs. Indirect Rollover: The Method Matters

There are two ways to move money from a 401(k) to an IRA, and they are not the same.

A **direct rollover** means the money moves directly from the employer plan to the IRA — either by a check made out to the new custodian or by electronic transfer. No money passes through your hands. No taxes are withheld. This is the cleaner method and what most people should request.

An **indirect rollover** means the plan writes the check to you. The problem: the plan is required to withhold 20% for federal income taxes at the time of distribution. You then have 60 days to deposit the full original amount — including the withheld 20%, which you would need to cover out of pocket — into an IRA. If you deposit only what you received (the 80%), the withheld 20% is treated as a taxable distribution and possibly subject to the early withdrawal penalty.

Most people do not know to ask for a direct rollover. Ask for it specifically.

## Retirement Income Considerations After a Rollover

Rolling your 401(k) into an IRA is the beginning of a new set of decisions, not the end. An IRA is an account — not a paycheck. At some point, you will need to decide how to draw from it, in what order relative to other accounts, and at what pace.

Those [retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/) are where the real work of retirement begins. For Long Beach residents who also have pension income, Social Security timing decisions, and potentially Roth accounts to coordinate, the income strategy is more layered than the rollover itself.

## Roth Conversion Considerations

Once your 401(k) is in a traditional IRA, you have the option to convert some or all of it to a Roth IRA. Roth conversions are taxable events — the converted amount is added to your ordinary income in the year of conversion — but future qualified Roth withdrawals are tax-free, and Roth accounts are not subject to Required Minimum Distributions during your lifetime.

The years between retirement and age 73, when RMDs begin, can represent a meaningful window for Roth conversion planning. Whether a conversion makes sense depends on your current income, your expected future income, and your California tax situation. Consult a qualified tax professional before pursuing this strategy. For more background, see [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/).

## Common 401(k) Rollover Mistakes

Even straightforward rollovers go sideways. These are the mistakes that come up most often.

### Missing the 60-Day Window on an Indirect Rollover

Life gets in the way. The 60-day clock runs from the date of distribution, not the date you decide to act. Miss it, and the distribution becomes taxable — potentially with a penalty.

### Rolling Over Without an Income Plan in Place

The rollover puts the money somewhere. It does not tell you when to use it, how much to take, or in what order relative to Social Security and other income. Treating the rollover as the finish line is one of the most common [retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/).

### Losing the Age-55 Early Withdrawal Exception

If you leave your employer in or after the year you turn 55, you can take distributions from that employer's 401(k) without the 10% early withdrawal penalty — even before 59½. Once you roll the money to an IRA, that exception is gone. The 59½ rule applies to IRAs.

### Rolling to an IRA Without Knowing What to Invest In

An IRA is an empty account until you choose investments. Many people roll money over and then leave it sitting in cash for months — or years — because they were not sure what to do next. This is worth addressing before you initiate the rollover.

### Cashing Out Instead of Rolling Over

This is the most expensive mistake, and it happens most often when someone does not know the rollover option exists or assumes it is more complicated than it is. The tax cost of a full cash-out on a large balance can be significant and is not recoverable.

### Evaluating the Rollover Without Considering the Pension Election

If you have a defined benefit pension — common among Long Beach-area Boeing, SCE, or public sector workers — the pension election decision and the 401(k) rollover decision are connected. The pension affects how much you need from the IRA in early retirement. See [pension distribution options](/pension-distribution-decisions/) before finalizing either decision.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I have to roll over my 401(k) when I retire from my job in Long Beach?

No. Rolling over is one option, not a requirement. You can leave the money in your former employer's plan if the plan allows it, roll to an IRA, roll to a new employer's plan, or take a distribution. The right choice depends on your age, your other income sources, the quality of your former employer's plan, and what you plan to do with the money in retirement. There is no deadline that forces the decision, though some plans do have rules for former employees after a certain period.

### What is the difference between a direct rollover and an indirect rollover?

With a direct rollover, the money moves from your employer plan straight to your IRA — you never touch it, and no taxes are withheld. With an indirect rollover, the plan sends the check to you, withholds 20% for federal taxes, and you have 60 days to deposit the full original amount (including the withheld 20%) into an IRA. If you can only deposit the 80% you received, the withheld 20% is treated as a taxable distribution. Requesting a direct rollover by name is generally the cleaner approach.

### Will I owe taxes on my 401(k) rollover?

Not at the time of the rollover itself, if it is done correctly as a direct rollover to a traditional IRA. A rollover from a traditional 401(k) to a traditional IRA is a tax-neutral event — the money remains in a pre-tax account. Taxes are due when you take distributions in retirement, at ordinary income tax rates. If you convert to a Roth IRA instead, the converted amount is taxable in the year of conversion. Consult a tax professional regarding your specific situation.

### Does California tax 401(k) rollovers?

California does not tax the rollover event itself when money moves from a 401(k) to a traditional IRA. However, California taxes IRA distributions as ordinary income when you take them. California does not recognize the federal Roth conversion rules identically, so the California tax treatment of Roth conversions can differ from the federal treatment. The California Franchise Tax Board treats most retirement distributions as ordinary income. Consult a tax professional familiar with California tax law before making large rollover or conversion decisions.

### Can I roll my 401(k) into a Roth IRA instead of a traditional IRA?

Yes, this is called a Roth conversion rollover — sometimes called a direct conversion. The amount rolled over is included in your ordinary income for the year of conversion and is subject to federal and California income tax. There is no early withdrawal penalty on a properly executed conversion, but the tax bill can be significant depending on the balance. Whether this makes sense depends on your current tax bracket, your expected future income, and your timeline. Consult a qualified tax professional before converting.

### What happens if I miss the 60-day rollover window?

If you received the distribution and do not deposit it into an IRA within 60 days, the IRS treats the full amount — including any amount that was withheld for taxes — as a taxable distribution. If you are under 59½, a 10% early withdrawal penalty also applies. In limited circumstances, the IRS will grant a waiver for the 60-day rule due to specific hardship events, but this is not guaranteed. The safest approach is to use a direct rollover and never take personal possession of the funds.

### I worked for the Port of Long Beach for 25 years. Should I leave my 401(k) in their plan or roll it to an IRA?

This depends on factors specific to your situation: the quality and cost of the plan's investment options, whether you are between ages 55 and 59½ (which may affect the early withdrawal penalty rules), your other income sources, and whether you want a financial advisor actively involved in managing the assets. There is no universal answer. What matters is evaluating the actual plan options and costs against the flexibility an IRA would offer. This is the kind of decision a [Retirement Transition Review](/request-review/) is designed to help you think through.

### How long do I have to decide what to do with my 401(k) after I retire?

There is no universal deadline. Most employer plans allow former employees to keep money in the plan indefinitely, though some plans have rules requiring distributions if the balance is below a certain threshold. The more relevant timeline is your own: if you will need income from the account soon, the decision is more urgent. If you are years away from needing the money and the plan options are solid, there may be no rush. The key is not to let the absence of a deadline become an excuse for not making a decision at all.
