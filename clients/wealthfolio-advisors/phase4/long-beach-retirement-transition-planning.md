# Retirement Transition Planning in Long Beach: What to Decide Before You Stop Working

---

## Direct Answer

Retirement transition planning means identifying and sequencing the decisions that determine how retirement actually works financially. For Long Beach residents, that typically includes: when to stop working, what to do with the employer 401(k), pension election if applicable, income structure, and Social Security timing. These decisions are connected — making one without considering the others is the most common planning mistake.

- Retirement transition planning is not the same as retirement income planning — the transition is about the sequence of decisions; the income plan is about what happens after

## The Retirement Transition Decision Sequence

Most retirement planning guides treat these decisions as a checklist. They are not — they are a sequence. Making the wrong decision first can limit your options on the ones that follow. Here is how the decision sequence typically unfolds.

### 1. When to Stop Working

The timing of retirement is sometimes voluntary and sometimes not. Early retirement packages, layoffs, health changes, and caregiving responsibilities all create situations where the timing is partly out of your control. When you have flexibility, the decision involves weighing income needs, Social Security timing, healthcare coverage before Medicare at 65, and the value of additional contribution years.

For people in pension-eligible positions, additional years of service can meaningfully affect the pension benefit calculation — which is another reason this first decision connects to what follows.

### 2. Pension Distribution Election (If Applicable)

For workers with a defined benefit pension — which includes many people who spent careers at Boeing, SCE, the City of Long Beach, the Port, or the school district — the pension distribution election is one of the most consequential financial decisions of the retirement transition.

Pension election structures typically involve tradeoffs between a higher monthly benefit for the retiree's life only versus a reduced benefit that continues to a surviving spouse. These elections are often irrevocable once made, and they arrive with deadlines from the employer or plan administrator.

Making this decision without understanding how the pension interacts with Social Security, your 401(k) or 403(b), and your spouse's income situation is one of the most common retirement mistakes in this region. The [pension distribution options](/pension-distribution-decisions/) page covers the decision factors in detail.

### 3. 401(k) Rollover Decision

When you leave an employer, you generally have several options for your 401(k): leave it in the current plan if the plan permits, roll it into an IRA, roll it into a new employer's plan, or take a lump-sum distribution. Each option has different tax treatment, different flexibility, and different long-term implications.

The rollover decision is not as time-sensitive as many people assume — you do not need to move the money immediately. What matters more than speed is making the decision with a clear understanding of where the money is going and what role it will play in your income plan.

[What to do with your old 401(k)](/old-401k-decisions/) is one of the most detailed resources on this site. It covers the options, the tradeoffs, and the questions to answer before you move anything.

### 4. Retirement Income Structure

After the rollover decision comes the question most people have not answered yet: how are you actually going to generate income from these assets?

A rollover moves money from one account to another. It does not create an income strategy. Turning accumulated savings into a reliable stream of retirement income requires deciding which accounts to draw from first, in what amounts, and with what tax implications. These choices affect how long your savings last and how much of your income goes to taxes each year.

[Retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/) goes deeper on this topic — including how Social Security, pension income, and account withdrawals layer together.

### 5. Social Security Filing Strategy

Social Security filing age affects your monthly benefit for the rest of your life. Claiming at 62 locks in a permanently reduced benefit. Waiting until 70 maximizes the monthly amount. The break-even math, the spousal benefit considerations, and the interaction with your other income sources all affect which timing makes sense.

Early claiming is not always wrong — but it is often done without a full analysis of the lifetime tradeoff. This decision is worth modeling carefully before you file, especially if you are in good health and have other income sources available in the early retirement years.

### 6. Healthcare Before Medicare

Medicare eligibility begins at 65. If you retire before 65, you need a bridge plan — typically COBRA continuation coverage from your former employer, a marketplace plan through Covered California, or coverage through a spouse's employer plan.

Healthcare costs in the pre-Medicare window are significant and are worth budgeting explicitly as part of the retirement income plan. Missing an enrollment window or underestimating costs in this period is a common and expensive mistake.

## Retirement Income Considerations

The transition decisions described above set the foundation. What follows is the income structure — how retirement actually works on a month-to-month basis.

For most Long Beach residents, retirement income comes from some combination of Social Security, 401(k) or IRA withdrawals, pension income if applicable, and possibly part-time or consulting income in the early years. California taxes most of these sources at ordinary income rates, with the notable exception of Social Security, which is not taxed at the state level in California.

The sequencing of withdrawals — which accounts to draw from first, how much, and when — has real tax consequences. It also affects Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs), which begin at age 73 for most retirement accounts. Missing an RMD carries significant tax penalties; consult a tax professional to understand how RMD timing applies to your accounts.

[Retirement income planning decisions](/retirement-income-decisions/) covers the income structure in depth, including how to think about withdrawal sequencing and tax layering across sources.

## Roth Conversion Considerations

For some people, the window between retirement and age 73 — before RMDs begin and often before Social Security is claimed at its maximum — represents a lower-income period that may be worth examining for Roth conversion opportunities.

A Roth conversion means moving funds from a traditional IRA or 401(k) into a Roth IRA. The amount converted is taxable in the year of conversion. The potential advantage: future growth and qualified withdrawals from the Roth account are tax-free, and Roth accounts are not subject to RMDs.

The right answer depends on your current tax rate, your projected tax rate in retirement, and your income picture in the conversion year. Large conversions can also affect Medicare premiums (through IRMAA) two years later. Consult a tax professional before converting. The [Roth conversion considerations](/roth-conversion-considerations/) page covers the evaluation framework.

## Common Retirement Transition Mistakes

These are the retirement transition mistakes WealthFolio Advisors sees most often — not as cautionary tales, but as reminders of where clarity makes the biggest difference.

### Making Pension Elections Under Deadline Pressure

Pension elections often arrive with a deadline and without much time for analysis. Many people choose the default or the first option without evaluating how it fits their overall income picture. Because these elections are typically irrevocable, a rushed choice here can have decades of consequences.

### Rolling Over the 401(k) Without an Income Plan

Moving the money to an IRA is a reasonable step — but many people stop there, treating the rollover as the completion of retirement planning rather than the beginning of it. The money is now in a different account. What it does from there still needs to be decided.

### Claiming Social Security Earlier Than Necessary

Claiming at 62 or 63 to get income flowing sooner is understandable, but it permanently reduces the monthly benefit. For people who have other income sources available in the early retirement years, delaying the claim is often worth modeling before deciding.

### Missing Retiree Medical Enrollment Windows

If your employer offers retiree medical coverage, there are enrollment windows that can close permanently. Missing them can mean losing access to coverage entirely, not just delaying it. This is particularly important to check in the final months before retirement.

### Ignoring the Tax Picture for the First Few Retirement Years

The early retirement years — before Social Security and RMDs — are often a lower-income window. People who do not use that window intentionally sometimes find themselves in higher tax brackets later, with fewer options.

[Retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/) covers these and others in more detail.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is retirement transition planning, and how is it different from general financial planning?

General financial planning covers a broad range of topics — budgeting, insurance, investments, estate planning — across your entire financial life. Retirement transition planning is focused specifically on the cluster of decisions that arise when you stop working: what to do with your employer accounts, how to elect pension benefits if applicable, when to file for Social Security, and how to structure income going forward. It is a defined phase with a defined set of decisions — not an ongoing relationship, though it can lead to one.

### How far in advance should I start planning for retirement in Long Beach?

Two to three years before your target retirement date is a reasonable window for meaningful planning. That timeline gives you room to evaluate Roth conversion opportunities, model Social Security timing, and understand how your pension election options interact with your other income sources. It also gives you time to address healthcare planning before Medicare at 65. Starting with 12 months to go is workable but compresses the decision space. Waiting until the month you retire almost guarantees that some decisions will be made under pressure.

### I worked for the City of Long Beach — do I have different retirement decisions than someone in the private sector?

Yes, in meaningful ways. City of Long Beach employees are typically covered by a defined benefit pension through CalPERS or the City's pension system. That means you are making a pension distribution election in addition to any 401(k) or 457(b) decisions — and those elections are often irrevocable. The interaction between pension income, Social Security (which some public employees are not covered by, depending on their position), and supplemental account withdrawals creates a more complex income picture than most private-sector retirees face. [Government employee retirement decisions](/government-retirement-transition-guide/) covers the public-sector retirement landscape in more detail.

### What happens to my 401(k) when I retire — do I have to roll it over right away?

No. You are not required to roll over your 401(k) immediately upon retirement. Most plans allow former employees to leave money in the plan for some period of time. The rollover decision is worth making thoughtfully, not quickly. The key questions are: what are the investment options and fees in the current plan versus a rollover IRA, when do you need to start drawing income from the account, and how does the rollover fit into your overall income structure? [What to do with your old 401(k)](/old-401k-decisions/) walks through the full decision.

### Can I retire in Long Beach before 65 and still have health coverage?

Yes, but it requires planning. Before Medicare at 65, your options typically include COBRA continuation coverage from your former employer (usually the most straightforward but often the most expensive), a marketplace plan through Covered California, or coverage through a working spouse's employer plan. Some Long Beach-area employers also offer retiree medical benefits, which is worth checking before you separate. Healthcare costs in the pre-Medicare window are significant — they belong in your retirement budget from the start, not as an afterthought.

### What are the most common retirement transition mistakes people make in their late 50s and 60s?

The most consequential mistakes tend to cluster around decisions made too quickly: pension elections made under deadline pressure without full analysis, 401(k) rollovers completed without an income strategy in place, and Social Security filed early without modeling the lifetime tradeoff. Missing retiree healthcare enrollment windows is another common and costly error. These are not mistakes made by uninformed people — they are mistakes made by people who did not know the full range of what they were deciding. [Retirement transition mistakes to avoid](/retirement-transition-mistakes/) covers these in depth.

### How does California tax income from a 401(k) rollover or IRA withdrawal?

A direct rollover — from a 401(k) to a traditional IRA — is generally not a taxable event, provided the funds move directly between institutions (or are redeposited within 60 days in an indirect rollover). Once the money is in the IRA, withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income at both the federal and California state level. California taxes retirement account withdrawals at ordinary income rates, which can be significant depending on the amount withdrawn and your other income in the same year. Consult a tax professional to understand how California income tax will apply to your specific withdrawal strategy.
